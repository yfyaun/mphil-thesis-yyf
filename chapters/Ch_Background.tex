\chapter{Background and Related Work}
\label{ch:background}

Sensing-assisted beam management brings together a communication decision, an
environmental observation process, and a user-specific control action.  A
useful review must therefore follow the dependencies between these elements,
rather than classify prior work according to the modules ultimately adopted by
one particular system.  This chapter begins with beam alignment in mobile V2I
networks, then considers sensing-assisted beam prediction and the wider
observability supplied by distributed cooperative perception.  It subsequently
examines the unresolved transition from perceived physical targets to
communication users in multi-candidate scenes.  That final transition also
provides the appropriate context for tracking and for evaluation in terms of
the rate--overhead consequence of an association decision.

\section{Beam Alignment in High-Mobility V2I Networks}
\label{sec:background-beam-alignment}

\subsection{Measurement-Based Beam Alignment}
\label{subsec:background-measurement-beam-alignment}

High-frequency V2I links rely on directional transmission to obtain adequate
array gain.  A serving base station (BS) must consequently identify a suitable
transmit--receive beam pair for each active user equipment (UE), and must renew
that choice as the propagation geometry evolves.  Codebook-based procedures
address this requirement through initial-access sweeps, reference-signal
measurements, and subsequent refinement.  Such measurements are indispensable
because they provide link-specific evidence, but their use consumes time and
frequency resources that would otherwise carry data.

The cost--reliability trade-off is pronounced on roads.  Vehicle motion changes
the relative geometry over short intervals, vehicles and roadside structures
can intermittently block important paths, and several users can occupy similar
angular sectors.  Measuring a broad set of beams frequently can reduce the
risk of retaining obsolete channel information, whereas a narrow or infrequent
search can miss a viable direction.  Predictive beamforming and beam-tracking
studies have therefore sought to exploit vehicle state, sensing returns, or
historical link information to reduce repeated exhaustive search
\cite{liu2020radarassistedpredictivebeamformingvehicular,
yuan2020bayesianpredictivebeamformingvehicular,
du2021integratedsensingcommunicationsv2i}.

This literature establishes an important distinction for the remainder of the
chapter.  A prediction can prioritise a small set of beams, but it does not by
itself verify the service beam used for transmission.  In a practical
beam-management procedure, communication-side measurements remain the means by
which the candidate set is confirmed, refined, or recovered.  The relevant
question is consequently not whether a learned model replaces measurement, but
whether it can reduce the measurement burden without imposing an unacceptable
loss of link reliability.

\subsection{Mobility, Beam Tracking, and Communication Performance}
\label{subsec:background-mobility-beam-tracking}

Mobility makes this question inherently temporal.  A beam selected from a
previous observation may become unsuitable because of displacement, a change
in orientation, or the emergence of blockage.  ISAC and learning-based
predictive beamforming methods address this issue by forecasting a future beam
or beamforming decision from radar echoes, channel histories, or estimated
vehicle state \cite{liu2022learningbasedpredictivebeamformingintegrated,
liu2022deepclstmpredictivebeamforming,Wang_2023}.  Their common objective is to
retain useful directional information while avoiding a full search at every
decision epoch.

The resulting policy cannot be judged by beam classification accuracy alone.
Candidate coverage indicates whether a short list contains a usable direction,
whereas measurement overhead records the radio resources required to identify
that direction.  The communication consequence additionally depends on
recovery events, the aging of channel information, and the resources remaining
for data transmission.  This observation motivates sensing-assisted beam
prediction as a candidate-reduction problem and raises two further questions:
what evidence should form the candidate ranking, and to which UE does that
evidence apply?

\section{Sensing-Assisted Beam Prediction}
\label{sec:background-sensing-prediction}

\subsection{Sensing Modalities and Prediction Outputs}
\label{subsec:background-sensing-modalities}

Sensing-assisted prediction uses the coupling between the physical scene and
wireless propagation.  Position information provides a coarse geometric cue,
while camera observations reveal semantic context and potential blockage.
Radar, LiDAR, and ISAC observations add range, angle, or motion information
that is more directly related to vehicle geometry.  Representative studies
have used cameras for beam and blockage prediction, vision and position jointly
for multimodal beam prediction, radar for beam inference, and LiDAR for future
beam prediction \cite{alrabeiah2019millimeterwavebasestations,
xu2022computervisionaidedmmwave,
charan2021visionpositionmultimodalbeamprediction,
demirhan2021radaraided6gbeam,
jiang2022lidaraidedfuturebeam}.  These results show that environmental
observations can reduce uncertainty before a radio measurement is performed.

The output of the predictor materially affects how this information can be
used.  A single-beam classifier is suitable when the task is posed as immediate
selection of one codebook index.  Ranked Top-$K$ predictions instead support a
candidate list, while a per-beam power or quality distribution retains both an
ordering and information about the concentration of the prediction.  The latter
is particularly useful when a policy must decide whether a small candidate set
is sufficiently credible to measure.  Accordingly, common evaluation measures
include Top-$K$ accuracy, received-power loss, candidate coverage, and beam
search overhead \cite{alkhateeb2023deepsense6glargescalerealworld,
charan2022multimodalbeampredictionchallenge}.

\subsection{Multimodal and Decision-Oriented Prediction}
\label{subsec:background-multimodal-prediction}

Recent work extends single-modality inference through multimodal fusion,
sequential modelling, and decision-oriented beam management.  Multimodal
transformer formulations learn complementary information from image, point
cloud, radar, and position inputs, while sequential BEV-based prediction and
occlusion-aware models exploit temporal context and scene structure
\cite{tian2023multimodaltransformerswirelesscommunications,
zeng2026bevfusionbasedframeworksequential,
orimogunje2026occlusionawaremultimodalbeamprediction}.  Reinforcement-learning
and contextual-bandit approaches further connect sensing-derived features to
beam-management decisions and communication objectives
\cite{ghassemi2024multimodaltransformerreinforcementlearningbased,
farzanullah2025beamselectionisacusing}.  Together, these studies strengthen the
case for representing beam prediction as ranked, uncertain evidence rather
than as a standalone label.

Nevertheless, a substantial proportion of this literature is organised around
an already identified link: a sensing sample is paired with a particular UE,
or the relevant transmitter is known from the task construction.  This
assumption is convenient for learning a beam mapping, but it is restrictive in
a road scene containing several visible vehicles.  The sensing system first
observes physical objects; it must still determine which observed object
corresponds to the UE represented by a communication measurement.  Improving
the quality of beam prediction alone does not resolve this ambiguity.  Before
addressing it directly, the next section considers whether the scene itself has
been observed with sufficient coverage and geometric consistency.

\section{Distributed Cooperative Perception in V2X Networks}
\label{sec:background-cooperative-perception}

\subsection{Multi-View Observability and Cooperative Perception}
\label{subsec:background-cooperative-observability}

Roadside sensing is limited by the field of view and viewpoint of each
individual sensor.  A camera can be obstructed by another vehicle or affected
by illumination and weather, whereas a geometric sensor can become sparse or
lose semantic detail.  In addition, a single observation point may not reveal
the vehicle surface, lane context, or visibility relation that is most relevant
to a serving BS.  Distributed sensing and cooperative perception address these
limitations by combining complementary views from multiple agents or
infrastructure nodes.

The cooperative-perception literature has shown that feature fusion across V2X
agents can enlarge the observable scene, while also exposing practical
challenges involving pose error, asynchronous sensing, heterogeneous agents,
and the communication cost of exchanging information
\cite{xu2022v2xvitvehicletoeverythingcooperativeperception}.  In a
communication setting, the target BS need not become a global fusion centre for
the entire road network.  It can instead use neighbouring observations to form
a more complete local description of the region in which it makes a
beam-management decision.  Distributed sensing-aided networking provides a
communication-oriented precedent by exchanging environment semantics from
multiple RGB sensing nodes for beam prediction
\cite{imran2024environmentsemanticcommunicationenabling}.

\subsection{BEV Representation for Heterogeneous Sensing}
\label{subsec:background-bev-representation}

A shared bird's-eye-view (BEV) representation offers a useful spatial interface
for such local fusion.  Camera features from arbitrary rigs can be lifted and
projected to a ground-plane representation, while point-cloud features can be
encoded in the same frame.  Lift-Splat-Shoot and BEVFusion demonstrate how this
type of representation accommodates multi-view cameras and complementary
geometric sensing \cite{philion2020liftsplatshootencoding,
liu2024bevfusionmultitaskmultisensorfusion}.  Point-based detectors such as
CenterNet further illustrate how a BEV feature map can support target-level
reasoning through vehicle centres and associated attributes
\cite{zhou2019objectspoints}.

For sensing-assisted communication, the value of BEV is not that an autonomous-
driving detection task becomes a communication decision.  Rather, a common
local frame can preserve the relationship among vehicles, surrounding
infrastructure, and the serving BS, while making heterogeneous and multi-view
evidence available at each detected target.  This is an appropriate basis for
beam-related inference because propagation and beam quality depend on the
target's local geometry and visibility.  Existing work on distributed sensing
and multimodal beam prediction demonstrates these complementary directions,
but generally does not jointly organise multiview roadside observations and
BS-local geometric sensing into unbound, target-level evidence for subsequent
user-specific beam management \cite{imran2024environmentsemanticcommunicationenabling,
zeng2026bevfusionbasedframeworksequential}.  The remaining issue is therefore
not only whether a vehicle can be detected and assigned a beam ranking, but
whether that ranking can be attributed to the correct communication user.

\section{Target-to-User Association in Multi-Candidate V2I Scenarios}
\label{sec:background-target-user-association}

\subsection{Target Ambiguity in Multi-Candidate Scenes}
\label{subsec:background-target-ambiguity}

Perception and communication use different notions of identity.  A perception
module produces physical-target detections, and a tracker later assigns
temporary track identifiers to those targets.  A communication system, by
contrast, obtains measurements and schedules resources by UE identity.  The
two entities can refer to the same vehicle, but their correspondence is not
provided by a generic sensing output.  The distinction becomes essential when
several candidate vehicles are visible, when they occupy similar angular
regions, or when a vehicle is briefly occluded or missed by the detector.

Multi-candidate beam-prediction work has begun to expose this issue.  Charan
\emph{et al.} consider transmitter identification as part of camera-based beam
prediction in multi-candidate V2I scenarios, while distributed semantic
communication includes object association-based tracking alongside beam
inference \cite{charan2023camerabasedmmwavebeam,
imran2024environmentsemanticcommunicationenabling}.  These studies make clear
that target-level inference cannot simply be equated with user-level beam
alignment.  However, transmitter identification, an oracle object label, or a
geometric proxy does not by itself specify how a sensed target should be
associated with a UE-level communication observation when multiple plausible
matches are present.

\subsection{Association and Temporal Tracking}
\label{subsec:background-association-tracking}

Target-to-user association is a data-association problem spanning two evidence
domains.  Sensing can supply a target location, motion state, and beam
likelihood, whereas communication measurements supply UE-indexed evidence about
the link.  A credible association must use their agreement while retaining the
option that no sensed target should be assigned.  This differs from treating
the output of a detector as a UE identity, and it explains why one-to-one
assignment, acceptance criteria, and re-association are relevant to
beam-management decisions.

Tracking provides the temporal component of this association problem.
Tracking-by-detection methods such as CenterTrack associate successive physical
observations using location and motion information \cite{zhou2020trackingobjectspoints}.
In the vehicular communication literature, radar-assisted, Bayesian, and ISAC
predictive beamforming approaches likewise use estimated vehicle state to
anticipate future directional decisions
\cite{liu2020radarassistedpredictivebeamformingvehicular,
yuan2020bayesianpredictivebeamformingvehicular,
du2021integratedsensingcommunicationsv2i}.  These methods establish the value
of state propagation, but commonly assume a direct relationship between the
tracked target and the communication link.

An interacting multiple-model (IMM) tracker is well suited to a road scene
because it can combine motion hypotheses for, for example, near-constant motion
and manoeuvring turns.  Its relevant role here is limited but important: it
maintains the state and temporal validity of an association after communication-
side evidence has anchored a target to a UE.  It does not infer UE identity
from visual appearance.  If a track becomes inconsistent with subsequent
detections or loses reliability, a beam-management procedure should request
new communication evidence and re-establish the association rather than apply
an old target's beam hint.

\subsection{Association-Aware Beam-Management Evaluation}
\label{subsec:background-association-aware-evaluation}

The quality of target-to-user association must ultimately be judged by its
effect on the communication procedure.  Detection accuracy and beam Top-$K$
coverage are necessary diagnostics, but neither indicates whether a candidate
list was delivered to the correct UE.  Association coverage, accepted-assignment
accuracy, track continuity, and re-association frequency expose the intermediate
failure modes.  Candidate recall, measurement overhead, effective rate,
lower-tail rate, and outage then show how those failures affect the user-level
outcome.

Existing multimodal datasets and realistic simulation frameworks make parts of
this chain observable by providing synchronised sensing and communication data
or by comparing resource-efficient prediction policies
\cite{alkhateeb2023deepsense6glargescalerealworld,
park2025resourceefficientbeampredictionmmwave}.  ISAC and learning-based
beam-management studies have also considered received power, spectral
efficiency, sum rate, regret, and latency
\cite{liu2022learningbasedpredictivebeamformingintegrated,
Wang_2023,ghassemi2024multimodaltransformerreinforcementlearningbased,
farzanullah2025beamselectionisacusing,mollah2026multimodalsensingfusionmmwave}.
Yet a high beam-prediction metric, or even a rate calculation under assumed
user correspondence, cannot establish whether sensing has yielded a valid
user-specific beam-management gain.  A resource-aware evaluation must preserve
the sequence from unbound target evidence, through association and fallback,
to the actual candidate measurements and user rate.  A 5G NR-oriented
measurement abstraction is useful for this purpose because it accounts for the
distinct roles of access, refinement, and data transmission without claiming to
reproduce every protocol detail.

\section{Research Gaps and Thesis Positioning}
\label{sec:background-gap}

The reviewed work establishes a coherent but incomplete chain.  Beam-prediction
studies show that sensing can prioritise candidate directions; cooperative
perception shows how multiple viewpoints and modalities can improve scene
observability; and tracking and predictive beamforming show that vehicle state
matters over time.  Multi-candidate scenes add a further interface: a beam
ranking attached to a physical target has communication value only after that
target has been associated with the intended UE.  The resulting association
must also remain credible when the observations evolve or become temporarily
unavailable.

Three connected gaps follow.  First, existing communication-oriented methods
provide limited treatment of distributed multimodal target-level scene evidence
that jointly exploits multi-view roadside observations and BS-local geometric
sensing.  Second, the link between a perceived target and a communication UE
is frequently assumed by data construction, position labels, or a known
transmitter; it is less often represented as a measurement-anchored, temporally
maintained association.  Third, evaluation is commonly centred on prediction
quality or on a rate objective that presupposes user correspondence, leaving
the propagation of association error to measurement overhead and effective user
performance insufficiently characterised.

This thesis addresses these gaps with a target-BS-centered framework.  It forms
unbound target-level beam evidence through distributed multimodal BEV
perception, uses communication-side evidence and IMM-based tracking to establish
and maintain target-to-user association, and evaluates the resulting candidate
measurement policy under a common resource model.  The framework does not
equate a sensing prediction with an immediately usable service beam: a hint is
used only when it has a credible user association, while conventional
measurement remains available for confirmation and recovery.  Chapter~\ref{ch:system-model}
formalises the corresponding entities, observations, and decision variables.
