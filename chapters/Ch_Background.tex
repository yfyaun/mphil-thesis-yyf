\chapter{Background and Related Work}
\label{ch:background}

Sensing-assisted beam management links a communication decision to an
observation of the physical environment.  The relevant literature is therefore
best understood through the sequence by which a BS acquires beam information:
it first aligns or tracks a directional link, may use sensing to narrow the
search, observes a road scene through one or more sensing nodes, and must then
apply target-level evidence to the intended communication UE.  This chapter
reviews the corresponding work in that order.  The final section identifies
the gaps that arise when these steps must operate together in a multi-candidate
V2I scene.

\section{Beam Alignment in High-Mobility V2I Networks}
\label{sec:background-beam-alignment}

\subsection{Conventional Beam Alignment}
\label{subsec:background-conventional-beam-alignment}

High-frequency V2I links use directional transmission to obtain sufficient
array gain.  The serving BS must consequently determine a suitable beam pair
for every active UE and renew that decision as the propagation geometry changes.
In codebook-based operation, initial access and later refinement obtain this
link-specific evidence through synchronisation and reference-signal
measurements.  Exhaustive search provides broad coverage but consumes a large
measurement budget.  Hierarchical search and restricted refinement reduce that
budget by probing a subset of the codebook, at the cost of relying on a coarse
or previously acquired direction.

This trade-off is particularly severe on roads.  Vehicle displacement changes
the departure and arrival angles over short intervals, while vehicles and
roadside structures can make a previously useful path unavailable.  A policy
that repeatedly measures a wide beam set reduces the risk of stale channel
knowledge but leaves fewer resources for data transmission.  Conversely, a
narrow search can fail when the prior direction is no longer valid.  Thus, the
function of an auxiliary prediction is not to declare a service beam without
measurement; it is to prioritise candidates that the communication procedure
can subsequently confirm or reject.

\subsection{Beam Tracking in Dynamic V2I Environments}
\label{subsec:background-dynamic-beam-tracking}

Beam tracking addresses the temporal limitation of a one-off alignment.  Liu
\emph{et al.} use roadside radar to estimate vehicle motion and support
predictive V2I beamforming, while explicitly considering the allocation of
sensing and communication power \cite{liu2020radarassistedpredictivebeamformingvehicular}.
Yuan \emph{et al.} estimate and predict vehicle state from dual-functional
radar--communication echoes, and use a Bayesian message-passing formulation to
obtain low-overhead predictive beamforming \cite{yuan2020bayesianpredictivebeamformingvehicular}.
Du \emph{et al.} model the vehicle as an extended target; their two-stage ISAC
scheme combines EKF state tracking with dynamic beamwidth control
\cite{du2021integratedsensingcommunicationsv2i}.

Other studies learn the temporal relation directly from communication-side
observations.  Liu \emph{et al.} use historical channel information in an
ISAC V2I setting to predict the next-slot beamforming matrix while incorporating
a sensing constraint \cite{liu2022learningbasedpredictivebeamformingintegrated}.
The Deep CLSTM model uses convolutional LSTM layers to capture the spatio-
temporal dependence needed for predictive beamforming
\cite{liu2022deepclstmpredictivebeamforming}.  Wang and Wong map reflected
signal samples directly to a beamforming vector and train the model with an
achievable-rate objective, thereby avoiding an explicit state-estimation stage
\cite{Wang_2023}.  Ma \emph{et al.} use historical camera and radar observations
in a CNN--GRU teacher to predict current and future beams, then distil that
knowledge into a lightweight student for beam tracking
\cite{ma2026knowledgedistillationlightweightmultimodal}.

These methods demonstrate that mobility can be exploited to reduce repeated
search.  Their state or sequence, however, is normally defined for an already
specified communication link.  In a multi-vehicle roadside scene, the BS must
first decide which physical target provides the state or sensing evidence for a
particular UE.  This distinction motivates sensing-assisted prediction and, in
later sections, target-to-user association.

\section{Sensing-Assisted Beam Prediction}
\label{sec:background-sensing-prediction}

\subsection{Vision- and Geometry-Aided Beam Prediction}
\label{subsec:background-vision-geometry-prediction}

Camera-based work established that external scene information can be useful
before a mmWave measurement is taken.  Alrabeiah \emph{et al.} use images from
cameras colocated with mmWave BSs, together with sub-6~GHz information, to
predict mmWave beams and blockage events \cite{alrabeiah2019millimeterwavebasestations}.
Xu \emph{et al.} extract vehicle size and position cues from visual observations
and use a neural model to infer a V2X beam pair; they additionally predict beam
coherence time to determine when realignment is needed
\cite{xu2022computervisionaidedmmwave}.

Geometry-oriented sensing supplies complementary range and motion cues.  On
the DeepSense 6G data, Demirhan and Alkhateeb use radar observations to infer
the mmWave beam and study their potential to reduce beam-training overhead
\cite{demirhan2021radaraided6gbeam}.  Jiang \emph{et al.} use LiDAR observations
to predict both current and future beams for real-world V2I links
\cite{jiang2022lidaraidedfuturebeam}.  These studies differ in their sensing
inputs, yet share the idea that environmental geometry can narrow the radio
search space before an explicit beam sweep.

\subsection{Multimodal Beam-Prediction Models}
\label{subsec:background-multimodal-beam-prediction}

Multimodal models seek to combine the complementary information retained by
these individual sensors.  Charan \emph{et al.} use camera observations and
position information from a real mmWave V2I dataset to predict beams, providing
an early vision--position fusion baseline \cite{charan2021visionpositionmultimodalbeamprediction}.
DeepSense 6G subsequently provided synchronised communication, camera, GPS,
LiDAR, and radar records for studying such mappings under common data
conditions \cite{alkhateeb2023deepsense6glargescalerealworld}.  The associated
multi-modal beam-prediction challenge placed particular emphasis on
generalisation across locations, times, and scenes rather than a single
in-distribution accuracy value \cite{charan2022multimodalbeampredictionchallenge}.

Model design has diversified beyond direct feature concatenation.  Tian
\emph{et al.} use a transformer to learn cross-modal and temporal relations
among image, point-cloud, radar, and GPS inputs for beam prediction
\cite{tian2023multimodaltransformerswirelesscommunications}.  Zeng \emph{et al.}
project camera, LiDAR, radar, and GPS information into a shared BEV
representation and apply a temporal transformer for sequential beam prediction
\cite{zeng2026bevfusionbasedframeworksequential}.  Orimogunje \emph{et al.}
combine RGB, LiDAR, radar range--angle information, GNSS, and short-term mmWave
power history in a multi-task model that estimates beams, blockage probability,
and two-dimensional pose \cite{orimogunje2026occlusionawaremultimodalbeamprediction}.
Wen \emph{et al.} introduce a mask-transformer architecture that explicitly
models missing modalities when predicting beams
\cite{wen2026amberadaptivemultimodalmask}.

Several models also place the predictor within a decision procedure.  Ghassemi
\emph{et al.} first use a multimodal transformer to predict a beam group and
then apply reinforcement learning to select a beam within that group
\cite{ghassemi2024multimodaltransformerreinforcementlearningbased}.  Farzanullah
\emph{et al.} combine multimodal ISAC features with a contextual-bandit policy
and transfer learning, evaluating beam selection through spectral-efficiency
regret \cite{farzanullah2025beamselectionisacusing}.  Yao \emph{et al.} formulate
beam management under location uncertainty as a vision-aided deep
reinforcement-learning problem \cite{yao2022jointsensingcommunicationsdeep}.
Mollah \emph{et al.} use cross-modal attention for connected-vehicle
beamforming, predict Top-$K$ beams, and report power-loss, latency, and
beam-search-overhead measures \cite{mollah2026multimodalsensingfusionmmwave}.

The output representation determines how a prediction can be consumed by beam
management.  A single-beam classifier is adequate when the task is defined as
one immediate codebook decision.  A ranked Top-$K$ output instead supports a
candidate set.  A beam-quality or power distribution retains both the candidate
ordering and the concentration of model evidence, which can be used to assess
whether a small candidate set is credible enough to measure.  Most of the
studies above, however, use a sensing sample that is already paired with a
communication link or a known transmitter.  In a multi-candidate scene, even a
correct target-level ranking does not establish that the ranking belongs to the
UE whose link will be measured.

\section{Distributed Cooperative Perception in V2X Networks}
\label{sec:background-cooperative-perception}

\subsection{Cooperative Perception in V2X Networks}
\label{subsec:background-cooperative-perception}

A single roadside sensor has a limited viewpoint.  Its observations can be
obstructed by other vehicles, degraded by weather or illumination, or unable to
show the context needed to distinguish nearby targets.  Cooperative perception
addresses this limitation by combining observations from multiple vehicles or
infrastructure nodes, but it must also account for calibration, synchronisation,
and the cost of information exchange.

V2X-ViT is a representative multi-agent approach: it uses a transformer-based
fusion mechanism for heterogeneous V2X observations and explicitly considers
pose error and asynchronous data \cite{xu2022v2xvitvehicletoeverythingcooperativeperception}.
Its objective is three-dimensional driving perception rather than beam
management, yet it shows why a target BS can benefit from neighbouring
viewpoints without acting as a global fusion point.  In a communication-focused
setting, Imran \emph{et al.} let distributed RGB sensing nodes transmit
environment semantics to a BS for beam prediction; their framework also
includes transmitter identification and object-association-based tracking
\cite{imran2024environmentsemanticcommunicationenabling}.  This work is an
important precursor for distributed sensing-aided networking, although its
input is visual semantics rather than a joint multi-view and geometric scene
representation.

\subsection{BEV Representation for Cooperative Perception}
\label{subsec:background-bev-representation}

BEV provides a common local frame in which information from different
viewpoints can be compared.  Lift-Splat-Shoot maps image features from arbitrary
camera rigs through a three-dimensional frustum and onto a BEV grid
\cite{philion2020liftsplatshootencoding}.  BEVFusion combines camera and LiDAR
features in a unified BEV representation and demonstrates that this space can
support multiple perception tasks \cite{liu2024bevfusionmultitaskmultisensorfusion}.
CenterNet represents objects as centre points and regresses their attributes,
providing a compact target-level detection formulation that can operate on a
spatial feature map \cite{zhou2019objectspoints}.

For sensing-assisted communication, the relevance of BEV is spatial rather than
task-specific.  A local BEV frame can preserve the geometry among the serving
BS, observed vehicles, and neighbouring sensing nodes while exposing multiview
evidence at each target location.  Such a representation can therefore support
both vehicle detection and beam-related inference, but a detection result does
not itself identify a UE or validate a service beam.  This limitation becomes
explicit in multi-candidate beam management.

\section{Target-to-User Association in Multi-Candidate V2I Scenarios}
\label{sec:background-target-user-association}

\subsection{Target Ambiguity in Multi-Candidate Scenes}
\label{subsec:background-target-ambiguity}

Perception and communication use different notions of identity.  A perception
module produces physical-target detections, and a tracker later assigns
temporary track identifiers to those targets.  A communication system obtains
measurements and allocates resources by UE identity.  The two identities may
refer to the same vehicle, but their correspondence is not supplied by a
generic sensing output.  The distinction matters when several vehicles are
visible, occupy similar angular regions, or are intermittently missed by the
detector.

Charan \emph{et al.} move camera-based beam prediction from a single-candidate
setting to multi-candidate V2I scenes by introducing transmitter identification
to select the communication target among visual candidates
\cite{charan2023camerabasedmmwavebeam}.  Imran \emph{et al.} similarly include
transmitter identification in their distributed environment-semantic framework
and use object association to support tracking
\cite{imran2024environmentsemanticcommunicationenabling}.  These studies show
that a physical target cannot simply be treated as a communication user.  They
do not, however, fully specify how a sensing target should be associated with a
UE-level communication measurement when several target--UE pairings remain
plausible.

\subsection{Association and Temporal Tracking}
\label{subsec:background-association-tracking}

Target-to-user association is a data-association problem spanning sensing and
communication evidence.  Sensing can provide a target location, motion state,
and beam likelihood, while communication measurements provide UE-indexed link
evidence.  A valid assignment must compare those sources, permit a target or UE
to remain unmatched, and be reconsidered when its support becomes unreliable.
This differs fundamentally from treating a detector output as a UE identity.

Tracking-by-detection supplies one part of this temporal mechanism.  CenterTrack
associates objects across frames by jointly predicting detections and their
displacements \cite{zhou2020trackingobjectspoints}.  The ISAC beam-tracking
methods in Section~\ref{sec:background-beam-alignment} show how an estimated
vehicle state can inform a directional decision, but they generally start from
a target whose communication link is already known.  An IMM tracker is useful
in the present setting because it can maintain alternative motion hypotheses,
such as near-constant motion and manoeuvring turns, after communication-side
evidence has anchored a perception track to a UE.  It maintains the temporal
validity of that association; it does not infer a UE identity from visual
appearance.  When track consistency or association confidence is lost, the
appropriate action is re-association using communication measurements rather
than continued use of an old beam hint.

\subsection{Association-Aware Beam-Management Evaluation}
\label{subsec:background-association-aware-evaluation}

Association quality must be evaluated through its downstream communication
effect.  Detection accuracy and beam Top-$K$ coverage diagnose whether a
physical target was observed and whether its candidate list is useful.  They do
not reveal whether that list was applied to the correct UE.  Association
coverage, accepted-assignment accuracy, track continuity, and re-association
frequency expose this intermediate failure mechanism.  Candidate recall,
measurement overhead, effective rate, lower-tail rate, and outage then quantify
the consequence for the user.

Park \emph{et al.} construct a realistic multimodal simulation framework and
use cross-modal relational knowledge distillation to transfer a multimodal
teacher to a radar-only beam-prediction student
\cite{park2025resourceefficientbeampredictionmmwave}.  Their study illustrates
how sensing assets and communication labels can be placed in a common
evaluation environment, although its focus is model compression rather than
target-to-user association.  The ISAC formulation of Liu \emph{et al.}
optimises predictive beamforming subject to a sensing constraint and evaluates
the resulting sum-rate trade-off
\cite{liu2022learningbasedpredictivebeamformingintegrated}.  The end-to-end
model of Wang and Wong evaluates its reflected-signal-based beamforming through
an achievable-rate objective \cite{Wang_2023}.  These rate-oriented studies
are essential, but both assume the link to which the sensing or channel state
belongs.

An association-aware evaluation must retain the entire decision chain: unbound
target evidence, target-to-user assignment, tracking or re-association,
candidate measurement, and data transmission.  A 5G NR-oriented measurement
abstraction is appropriate because it distinguishes initial access, beam
refinement, candidate measurement, and data resources without claiming to
reproduce every protocol-level detail.  It can therefore reveal whether a
reduction in measurement burden remains beneficial after association errors and
fallback events are accounted for.

\section{Research Gaps and Thesis Positioning}
\label{sec:background-gap}

The reviewed work establishes a coherent but incomplete chain.  Conventional
alignment and predictive tracking show why directional V2I communication needs
timely beam information.  Vision-, radar-, LiDAR-, and multimodal models show
that sensing can rank beam candidates.  Cooperative perception and BEV
representations show how the observable road scene can be enlarged and made
spatially consistent.  In multi-candidate scenes, however, the beam evidence
attached to a physical target becomes useful only after it is associated with
the intended communication UE and remains credible over time.

Three connected gaps follow.  First, communication-oriented methods provide
limited treatment of distributed multimodal target-level scene evidence that
jointly exploits multi-view roadside observations and BS-local geometric
sensing.  Second, the link between a perceived target and a communication UE
is often supplied by data construction, a position label, or a known
transmitter, rather than represented as a measurement-anchored association that
is maintained through motion and observation interruption.  Third, existing
evaluation commonly reports prediction quality or a rate objective under an
assumed user correspondence, leaving the propagation of association errors to
measurement overhead and effective user performance insufficiently
characterised.

This thesis addresses these gaps with a target-BS-centered framework.  It forms
unbound target-level beam evidence through distributed multimodal BEV
perception, uses communication-side evidence and IMM-based tracking to establish
and maintain target-to-user association, and evaluates the resulting candidate-
measurement policy under a common resource model.  The framework does not
equate a sensing prediction with an immediately usable service beam: a hint is
used only when it has a credible user association, while conventional
measurement remains available for confirmation and recovery.  Chapter~\ref{ch:system-model}
formalises the corresponding entities, observations, and decision variables.
