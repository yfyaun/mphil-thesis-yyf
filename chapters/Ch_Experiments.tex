\chapter{Experimental Evaluation}
\label{ch:experiments}

This chapter evaluates the proposed multimodal sensing-assisted beam-management
framework.  The analysis follows the information flow of the
framework: multimodal observations first support target-level detection and beam
prediction; a reliability-gated target-to-user association then determines
whether a sensing-derived candidate set is used; and the resulting measurement
policy is assessed through all-UE effective throughput and CSI-RS overhead.  The
chapter therefore separates learning-level evidence from its communication-level
consequences, rather than interpreting a beam-prediction accuracy alone as a
rate result.

\section{Multimodal V2I Dataset Construction}
\label{sec:experiments-dataset}

\subsection{Multimodal Simulation and Data Generation}
\label{subsec:experiments-data-generation}

The experiments use a simulated multimodal V2I dataset comprising synchronised
multiview camera images, BS-local ISAC point clouds, vehicle annotations,
trajectories, and ray-traced channels.  These observations are organised into
target-BS-centred sensing samples and temporally aligned beam-management
episodes.

At a sensing epoch, the target BS receives its local camera observation, camera
observations from neighbouring sensing nodes, and a BS-local ISAC point cloud.
These inputs form the target-BS-centred multimodal observation used by the
perception network.  Each beam-management episode couples these observations to
the corresponding link evolution, beam labels, and vehicle trajectories.  The
perception, association, measurement, and resource-accounting stages therefore
operate on a common scene instance, while online decisions exclude ground-truth
VUE identities.

A sensing sample is indexed by environmental condition, sensing instant, and
target BS.  Link-level labels additionally identify the corresponding VUE for
supervision and evaluation.  The 48-beam SSB codebook and 192-beam CSI
codebook are linked through their parent relation.  The perception network
produces a 192-way beam-class posterior for an observed physical target.  Its
ranking is used to score association hypotheses and to reduce the CSI-RS
candidate set; it is neither a UE identifier nor a direct service-beam decision.

\subsection{Scenario Definition and Data Splits}
\label{subsec:experiments-scenario}

The data contain a four-BS roadside scenario under the \emph{Clear},
\emph{Rain/Fog}, and \emph{Night} conditions.  Each target BS serves a local
region and receives complementary observations from neighbouring sensing nodes.
Mutually disjoint training, validation, and test partitions are used throughout
the learning and system-level evaluations.  Target tracks and
target-to-user associations are maintained locally at each target BS; the
experiments do not assume a global identity that persists across serving cells.

All system-level comparisons use a common radio configuration: a 100-ms SSB
periodicity, a 20-ms CSI-RS periodicity, a four-beam conventional refinement
within each SSB parent, and common ray-tracing segment, Doppler, power, noise,
scheduling,
regularised zero-forcing (RZF), and resource-accounting assumptions.  The
resource model is a 5G NR-oriented abstraction rather than a full PRB- and
RE-level protocol implementation.  In the Rain/Fog condition, the
association-margin threshold is fixed at \(\tau_{\mathrm{margin}}=0.10\).
Because this operating point was selected for the Rain/Fog test condition, the
corresponding results characterise that operating point rather than independent
out-of-sample generalisation.

\section{Evaluation Metrics and Protocol}
\label{sec:experiments-metrics}

\subsection{Detection and Beam-Prediction Metrics}
\label{subsec:experiments-learning-metrics}

Target detection is evaluated by AP@2m and Recall@2m.  The distance qualifier
requires a predicted target to lie within 2~m of its reference target, thereby
making the availability of a useful target-level observation explicit.  For
matched detections, Beam Top-1@2m and Beam Top-4@2m quantify whether the
strongest-beam class is retained by the predicted ranking.  The Top-4 power
ratio reports the fraction of reference link energy captured by the four
highest-ranked CSI beams.  It measures candidate-set utility, not calibration of
the beam-class posterior as a physical power distribution.

The reported learning schemes differ in modality and fusion mechanism:
Camera, ISAC, single-station multimodal, node-mean fusion, and cross-agent
multimodal.  They are compared under the same
target-level task and Top-1 cross-entropy beam supervision.  These
comparisons identify how different observations affect detection and ranked beam
candidates; they do not, by themselves, establish a communication-rate gain.

\subsection{System-Level Evaluation Protocol}
\label{subsec:experiments-system-protocol}

The system metric is the all-UE time-average effective throughput,
\begin{equation}
\bar{R}_{\mathrm{eff}}
 =
 \frac{1}{|\mathcal{U}|}
 \sum_{u\in\mathcal{U}}
 \left(
 \frac{1}{N_u}\sum_{n=1}^{N_u} r_{u,n}^{\mathrm{eff}}
 \right),
\label{eq:experiments-effective-rate}
\end{equation}
where \(r_{u,n}^{\mathrm{eff}}\) includes the effect of control, SSB, CSI-RS,
data allocation, and unscheduled intervals at evaluation instant \(n\).
Accordingly, an unserved interval contributes zero throughput.  This definition
prevents conditional scheduled-user rates from obscuring the resource cost of a
measurement policy.

The accompanying operational metrics are CSI-RS overhead, sensing-use ratio,
conventional-fallback ratio, and the mean number of measured candidates.  A
sensing-use event requires a currently observed target and a credible
target-to-user association.  Fallback is therefore an observable consequence of
the association and reliability gate, but it is not a substitute for a
standalone target-to-user association-accuracy metric, which is not reported
separately here.

The system comparison contains conventional four-beam refinement, conventional
twelve-beam refinement, and the perception-assisted policy.  All three use the
same codebooks, scheduling, RZF processing, and resource configuration.  The
perception-assisted policy scans a sensing-derived candidate set only after the
reliability test; otherwise, it executes conventional refinement.  The tracker
comparison applies IMM, KF-CV, and KF-CT to the same Top-1 CE detector and
system protocol.

fusion scheme is uniformly best across all metrics.  Cross-agent multimodal
fusion yields the highest AP@2m, Recall@2m, and Top-4 power ratio, at

target availability does not necessarily yield the strongest beam ranking on
every metric; candidate quality is therefore assessed using both Top-1 and

\subsection{Value of Nearby Sensing Nodes}
\label{subsec:experiments-nearby-nodes}

Figures~\ref{fig:experiments-node-ap}--\ref{fig:experiments-node-beam} vary the
number of available nearby sensing nodes from zero to five for fixed trained
models.  They are consequently missing-node robustness and coverage curves,
rather than comparisons between models retrained at different node counts.

For cross-agent attention, AP@2m rises from \(38.59\%\) with no nearby node to
\(80.41\%\) with five nearby nodes; Recall@2m rises from \(52.34\%\) to
\(83.15\%\).  In contrast, Beam Top-4@2m is already high with few nodes and
then approaches saturation.  The contrast indicates that additional viewpoints
first improve target visibility and localisation.  Their effect on the exact
Top-1 beam ranking is more method dependent, which is consistent with the
different roles of target-BS geometry, modality fusion, and cross-node
observations.

\section{Reliability-Gated Beam Management}
\label{sec:experiments-beam-management}

\subsection{Results Across Weather Conditions}
\label{subsec:experiments-weather-system}

Table~\ref{tab:experiments-system-weather} and
Figure~\ref{fig:experiments-weather-rate} compare the three measurement
policies under each weather condition.  In Clear conditions, the
perception-assisted policy reaches an effective user rate of \(155.453\) Mbps
with \(1.39\%\) CSI-RS overhead, compared with \(151.367\) Mbps and
\(1.90\%\) for conventional four-beam refinement.  The twelve-beam policy
incurs \(5.71\%\) CSI-RS overhead and attains \(127.033\) Mbps.

The sensing-use entries and their complementary fallback ratios clarify how
the policy reaches these outcomes.  Sensing candidates are used for \(76.35\%\) of the Clear evaluation
instances, \(56.65\%\) of the Rain/Fog instances, and \(30.07\%\) of the Night
instances.  The corresponding fallback ratios are \(23.65\%\), \(43.35\%\), and
\(69.93\%\).  Thus, when the available observation and association evidence
becomes less reliable, the gate increasingly returns to conventional
refinement instead of applying sensing-derived beam information to a UE
unconditionally.

\subsection{Macro-Average Resource and Rate Trade-off}
\label{subsec:experiments-macro-system}

Figure~\ref{fig:experiments-system-tradeoff} aggregates the three weather
conditions with equal weight.  The perception-assisted policy achieves an
effective user rate of \(146.332\) Mbps with \(1.56\%\) CSI-RS overhead.  The
corresponding values are \(142.248\) Mbps and \(1.90\%\) for conventional
four-beam refinement, and \(115.890\) Mbps and \(5.71\%\) for conventional
twelve-beam refinement.

The perception-assisted operation remains explicitly conditional: its
macro-average sensing-use ratio is \(54.35\%\), while \(45.65\%\) of instances
use conventional fallback.  Taken together, the rate and overhead values show
the consequence of using sensing only when a current detection and a credible
target-to-user association are available.  The service beam is still selected
from the actually measured CSI-RS candidates.

\section{Tracking Performance}
\label{sec:experiments-tracking}

\subsection{End-to-End Tracker Comparison}
\label{subsec:experiments-tracker-system}

Table~\ref{tab:experiments-trackers} evaluates the three trackers with the same
Top-1 CE detector and system configuration.  Their effective rates
are close: \(146.332\) Mbps for IMM, \(146.373\) Mbps for KF-CV, and
\(145.880\) Mbps for KF-CT.  KF-CV is marginally higher than IMM in this
metric.  The table therefore does not support an effective-rate claim of
universal IMM superiority.

Effective rate alone does not characterise the role of the tracker.  IMM uses
sensing candidates in \(54.35\%\) of evaluated instances, with a mean of
\(2.651\) candidates and the same macro-average handover count as the two
reference filters.  IMM is retained in the proposed framework for its
multi-model state-estimation role in association maintenance; the table shows
that its end-to-end consequence must be interpreted jointly with candidate use,
fallback, and the upstream detector, rather than through a single tracker-only
metric.

\section{Discussion and Scope}
\label{sec:experiments-discussion}

The results support a consistent evidence chain.  Multimodal and nearby-node
observations improve different aspects of target availability and beam ranking;
the reliability gate determines whether this evidence can be safely converted
into a narrower CSI-RS measurement action; and the system evaluation reports
the resulting effective throughput together with the associated measurement
cost.  The substantial fallback share under Rain/Fog and Night is therefore an
integral part of the reliability-aware operating policy and should be
interpreted together with the average-rate result.

The system analysis characterises association-mediated operation through the
sensing-use ratio, fallback ratio, and all-UE effective rate; accepted-association
accuracy, lower-tail rate, and outage are outside the reported analysis.  The
conclusions are further limited to a local target-BS service region, a simulated
multimodal dataset, approximately 100-ms ray-tracing channel segments, and the
stated RZF and resource model.  They therefore describe performance under the
specified system assumptions rather than a complete 5G NR protocol stack or a
direct real-world deployment.
