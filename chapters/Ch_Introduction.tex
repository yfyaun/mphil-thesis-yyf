\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:introduction-background}

Vehicular networks are expected to support connected mobility and intelligent
transportation services whose data-rate and latency requirements are difficult
to meet with conventional wide-beam links. Millimetre-wave and other
high-frequency bands offer the required bandwidth, but their gains rely on
narrow directional beams. In vehicle-to-infrastructure (V2I) communication, a
base station (BS) must therefore maintain beam alignment while the relative
angle, range, blockage state, and dominant propagation paths of a vehicle change
rapidly \cite{xu2022computervisionaidedmmwave,liu2020radarassistedpredictivebeamformingvehicular}. Beam alignment is consequently a recurrent decision problem rather
than a one-off access operation.

Current high-frequency systems obtain direct communication evidence through
synchronisation signal block (SSB) sweeps, channel-state-information
reference-signal (CSI-RS) measurements, and subsequent channel-state-information
acquisition and refinement. These procedures are indispensable at initial access
and whenever the existing link is uncertain. Their cost, however, increases with
the beam-search space and the frequency of remeasurement. In a dynamic road
scene, a measurement may no longer describe the beam condition at the time of
data transmission; repeated exhaustive scans reduce this uncertainty at the
expense of time--frequency resources that could otherwise carry payload data.
The central trade-off is thus between measurement reliability and measurement
cost, rather than between alternative beam-index classifiers alone
\cite{charan2021visionpositionmultimodalbeamprediction,alkhateeb2023deepsense6glargescalerealworld,charan2023camerabasedmmwavebeam}.

Environmental sensing offers a route to narrowing the measurement search space.
Cameras reveal vehicle appearance, road geometry, and visibility, whereas
radar-, LiDAR-, and integrated sensing and communication (ISAC)-like
observations provide geometric and motion-related cues. These modalities have
been used to assist blockage prediction, current-beam selection, and
future-beam prediction \cite{alrabeiah2019millimeterwavebasestations,demirhan2021radaraided6gbeam,jiang2022lidaraidedfuturebeam}. More recent work has explored multimodal fusion and
predictive beamforming, reinforcing the premise that physical-scene observations
can guide communication measurements \cite{tian2023multimodaltransformerswirelesscommunications,liu2022learningbasedpredictivebeamformingintegrated,farzanullah2025beamselectionisacusing}.

For roadside V2I systems, the sensing opportunity is inherently spatially
distributed. The serving BS may observe the local road region through multiview
cameras and a BS-local ISAC point cloud, while neighbouring sensing nodes (SNs)
can expose road segments that are distant, weakly visible, or occluded from the
serving BS. A target-BS-centered bird's-eye-view (BEV) representation provides
a common local frame in which these heterogeneous observations can be aligned.
BEV perception has become an effective means of fusing visual and geometric
information across sensors and agents \cite{philion2020liftsplatshootencoding,liu2024bevfusionmultitaskmultisensorfusion,xu2022v2xvitvehicletoeverythingcooperativeperception}. In the present setting, it supplies a
communication-oriented representation from which vehicle targets and their
beam-quality rankings can be inferred. This motivates a distributed multimodal
BEV perception model that jointly addresses vehicle detection and beam
prediction, rather than treating them as unrelated tasks.

This sensing opportunity also exposes a distinction that is often hidden in
beam-prediction formulations. Sensing observes physical vehicles, whereas the
communication system serves user equipment (UE) identities. A beam-quality
ranking can be accurate for a detected vehicle but still be unusable if it is
applied to another UE. The problem is therefore not merely to predict promising
beams from a road scene. It is to use distributed multimodal sensing to produce
beam hints that are associated with, and remain attributable to, the correct
communication user. This distinction motivates the challenges and research
objectives developed below.

\section{Challenges}
\label{sec:introduction-challenges}

The first challenge is \emph{condition-aware distributed multimodal BEV
perception}. A single sensing modality or sensing site has unavoidable blind
spots. Camera observations are affected by field of view, illumination, weather,
and occlusion; point-cloud observations can be sparse or incomplete; and the
serving BS may not observe all relevant road segments. The technical issue is
therefore not simply to aggregate additional inputs. It is to align the semantic
information from multiview images, the geometric information from ISAC sensing,
and the complementary spatial coverage of neighbouring SNs in a target-BS local
representation. That representation must remain informative for vehicle
detection and conditional beam-quality ranking across variations in visibility,
blockage, traffic configuration, and weather.

The second challenge is \emph{sensing target-to-user association}. Existing
sensing-aided beam-prediction studies commonly evaluate samples for which the
sensing observation and communication link are already paired. In an operational
multi-vehicle V2I scene, however, the perception module returns a set of
physical targets while SSB and CSI-RS measurements are indexed by UEs. These
sets may differ because a visible vehicle need not be an active UE, an active UE
may be temporarily unobserved, and nearby vehicles may have similar spatial or
beam characteristics. A correct beam ranking assigned to the wrong UE is an
incorrect beam-alignment action. Sensing target-to-user association is therefore
the essential interface between environment perception and user-specific
communication, rather than a post-processing detail. This interface constitutes
a substantive distinction from beam-alignment formulations that presuppose link
correspondence at the sensing input \cite{charan2023camerabasedmmwavebeam,imran2024environmentsemanticcommunicationenabling}.

The third challenge is \emph{temporal correspondence maintenance}. The
association established at an access instant can be invalidated by mobility,
short-term occlusion, missed detection, and close interactions among vehicles.
The required mechanism is not visual recognition of a UE identity. Instead, it
must preserve the correspondence between an unbound perception track and a
communication UE through multi-object state estimation and gated data
association, while invoking a new communication measurement when the evidence
is no longer credible. This interpretation places tracking on a precise academic
footing: it is a mechanism for maintaining the validity of the target-to-user
correspondence on which a beam hint relies. It is related to both multi-object
tracking and predictive beamforming, but is evaluated here through its effect on
identity-consistent beam alignment \cite{zhou2020trackingobjectspoints,yuan2020bayesianpredictivebeamformingvehicular,liu2022deepclstmpredictivebeamforming}.

The final challenge is \emph{reliability-aware candidate reduction}. Sensing
should not replace communication measurement whenever a predicted Top-\(K\)
list is available. A candidate set can be misleading when the current target is
poorly observed, the target-to-user association is ambiguous, or the predicted
beam distribution is diffuse. Conversely, reducing the CSI-RS candidate set is
valuable only when the retained beams preserve sufficient quality for the
intended UE. A valid sensing-assisted policy must therefore accept a beam hint
only under credible correspondence and current sensing evidence, and otherwise
fall back to conventional refinement. Its merit must be established jointly by
candidate recall, CSI-RS overhead, effective rate, lower-tail rate, and outage;
Top-\(K\) accuracy alone cannot establish a communication benefit. This also
requires a system-level simulator that applies the same 5G NR-oriented
beam-management events and resource accounting to every policy under comparison.

Together, these challenges define the scope of this thesis: broad and
condition-aware environmental perception is necessary to generate useful beam
hints, while sensing target-to-user association and temporal correspondence
maintenance are necessary to apply those hints to the correct communication
links.

\section{Research Objective}
\label{sec:introduction-objective}

This thesis addresses the foregoing challenges through an integrated,
BS-centric sensing-to-communication framework. Its objective is to transform
distributed multimodal observations into beam-management information that is
useful for the correct communication UE, and to determine the communication
value of that information under a common measurement model. The framework is
organised around three connected objectives, which directly motivate the three
contributions of this thesis.

The first objective is to establish \emph{distributed multimodal BEV perception
for joint vehicle detection and beam prediction}. The target BS combines its
local sensing with complementary observations from neighbouring SNs in a common
BEV representation. This objective addresses the limited observability of a
single sensing site or modality: vehicle detection identifies where relevant
physical targets are located, while beam prediction estimates how the beam
energy is distributed across the codebook for each target. The resulting beam
distribution is used as a candidate-ranking signal, rather than as a direct
replacement for communication measurement.

The second objective is to realise \emph{target-to-user association with
IMM-based tracking}. It addresses the fact that a physical sensing target and a
communication UE are distinct entities whose relationship cannot be assumed in
a multi-vehicle scene. Communication-side measurements provide the evidence for
establishing an initial target-to-user association, while IMM-based tracking
maintains the association as vehicles move and observations evolve. The purpose
is to preserve the applicability of a beam hint to its intended UE, not to infer
communication identity from perception alone.

The third objective is to construct a \emph{Multimodal V2I Simulator and 5G
NR-Oriented Beam-Management Evaluation Framework}. Built on the available
multimodal V2I data assets, this system-level environment evaluates how
distributed perception and target-to-user association affect beam-management
decisions when sensing evidence is reliable or unreliable. It provides a common
setting for comparing candidate measurement, conventional fallback, and their
respective communication consequences. The aim is to expose the trade-off among
measurement overhead, link reliability, and communication performance, rather
than to equate a beam-prediction accuracy metric with an end-to-end system gain.

The framework is scoped to a target-BS-centered local service region. A vehicle
entering a new serving cell establishes a new local association; no global
multi-BS identity is assumed. Likewise, ground-truth identities and detections
are reserved for diagnostic or upper-bound analyses, whereas the formal decision
path uses sensing and communication information available to the BS. Detailed
codebook configurations, channel assumptions, scheduling rules, and physical-
layer metrics are specified in the system model and experimental evaluation.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:introduction-contributions}

The foregoing challenges require an integrated sensing-to-communication design,
rather than a collection of independent prediction modules. Accordingly, this
thesis contributes three mutually reinforcing research components that turn
environmental observations into identity-consistent beam-alignment decisions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Distributed Multimodal BEV Perception for Joint Vehicle
    Detection and Beam Prediction.} A target-BS-centered BEV perception model is
    developed to fuse camera observations from the target BS and neighbouring
    SNs with a BS-local ISAC point cloud. The model jointly detects vehicles and
    predicts the CSI beam-quality distribution associated with each target. Its
    distributional beam-prediction output represents the energy ranking across
    codebook beams, providing a candidate-ranking interface for uncertainty
    characterisation and subsequent communication decisions.

    \item \textbf{Target-to-User Association with IMM-Based Tracking.} An
    identity-consistent association mechanism is introduced to connect unbound
    perception targets with communication UEs. Conventional measurements support
    initial and recovery target-to-user association, while an interacting
    multiple-model (IMM) tracker maintains the association as targets evolve
    over time. One-to-one matching, SSB-parent consistency, and reliability
    assessment determine whether a sensing-derived beam hint remains applicable
    to its intended UE.

    \item \textbf{Multimodal V2I Simulator and 5G NR-Oriented Beam-Management
    Evaluation Framework.} A system-level simulator is established on the
    existing multimodal V2I data assets to evaluate sensing-assisted policies
    under a common 5G NR-oriented measurement and resource model. It relates
    perception and association quality to candidate recall, CSI-RS overhead,
    RZF-based effective rate, lower-tail rate, and outage, thereby distinguishing
    a useful beam ranking from a correctly attributed hint that provides a
    communication benefit.
\end{enumerate}

The contributions are stated at the formulation, method, and evaluation levels.
Quantitative comparative claims are restricted to results obtained under the
corresponding frozen protocol and held-out evaluation.

\section{Thesis Organization}
\label{sec:introduction-organization}

The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows. Chapter~\ref{ch:background}
reviews vehicular beam management, sensing-aided beam prediction, ISAC
predictive beamforming, BEV and cooperative perception, and sensing
target-to-user association. It establishes the related-work basis for
condition-aware multimodal sensing and identity-consistent beam alignment.

Chapter~\ref{ch:system-model} defines the target-BS local coordinate system,
the multimodal observations, the physical-target, perception-track, and
communication-UE identities, the target-to-user correspondence, and the
codebook, candidate-measurement, and resource models. Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}
then presents the proposed framework, including distributed multimodal BEV
perception, joint vehicle detection and beam prediction, target-to-user
association with IMM-based tracking, reliability-aware candidate scanning, and
the communication interface.

Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments} describes the data protocol, comparison methods,
layered metrics, and the Multimodal V2I Simulator with its 5G NR-oriented
beam-management evaluation framework. It examines how distributed multimodal
perception, target-to-user association with IMM-based tracking, and candidate
measurement decisions affect communication performance under common resource
assumptions. Finally, Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} summarises the findings
supported by the evaluation, discusses the limitations of the local BS scope and
communication abstraction, and outlines extensions towards cross-BS
correspondence, measurement calibration, and more detailed protocol-level
assessment.
