\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:introduction-background}

Vehicular networks are expected to support connected mobility and intelligent
transportation services whose data-rate and latency requirements are difficult
to meet with conventional wide-beam links.  Millimetre-wave and other
high-frequency bands offer the required bandwidth, but their gains rely on
narrow directional beams.  In vehicle-to-infrastructure (V2I) communication, a
base station (BS) must therefore maintain beam alignment while the relative
geometry and propagation conditions of a vehicle change rapidly.  Vision-aided
V2X beam alignment has, for example, used visual vehicle information to infer a
beam pair and predict its coherence time \cite{xu2022computervisionaidedmmwave}.
Beam alignment is consequently a recurrent decision problem rather than a
one-off access operation.

Current high-frequency systems obtain direct communication evidence through
synchronisation signal block (SSB) sweeps, channel-state-information
reference-signal (CSI-RS) measurements, and subsequent channel-state-information
acquisition and refinement.  These procedures are indispensable at initial
access and whenever the existing link is uncertain.  Their cost, however,
increases with the beam-search space and the frequency of remeasurement.  In a
dynamic road scene, a measurement can become stale before data transmission;
repeated exhaustive scans reduce that uncertainty at the expense of
time--frequency resources that could otherwise carry payload data.  The central
trade-off is therefore between measurement reliability and measurement cost,
rather than between alternative beam-index classifiers alone.

Environmental sensing offers a route to narrowing the measurement search
space.  Alrabeiah \emph{et al.} use BS-mounted cameras and sub-6~GHz side
information to predict mmWave beams and blockage events
\cite{alrabeiah2019millimeterwavebasestations}.  Demirhan and Alkhateeb use
radar observations to infer mmWave beams and examine the potential reduction in
beam-training overhead \cite{demirhan2021radaraided6gbeam}.  Jiang \emph{et al.}
use LiDAR observations to predict current and future beams in real-world V2I
links \cite{jiang2022lidaraidedfuturebeam}.  These studies demonstrate that
physical-scene observations can guide which communication measurements deserve
priority.

For roadside V2I systems, the sensing opportunity is inherently spatially
distributed.  The serving BS may observe the local road region through multiview
cameras and a BS-local ISAC point cloud, while neighbouring sensing nodes (SNs)
can reveal road segments that are distant, weakly visible, or occluded from the
serving BS.  A target-BS-centered bird's-eye-view (BEV) representation provides
a common local frame in which these observations can be aligned.
Lift-Splat-Shoot demonstrates how multi-camera features can be projected to a
BEV grid \cite{philion2020liftsplatshootencoding}.  BEVFusion shows how camera
and LiDAR features can be fused in a unified BEV representation
\cite{liu2024bevfusionmultitaskmultisensorfusion}.  V2X-ViT further demonstrates
the value of fusing observations from multiple V2X agents while accounting for
asynchrony and pose error \cite{xu2022v2xvitvehicletoeverythingcooperativeperception}.
In the present setting, a local BEV representation is used to produce
vehicle-level evidence for both detection and beam prediction.

This sensing opportunity also exposes a distinction that is often hidden in
beam-prediction formulations.  Sensing observes physical vehicles, whereas the
communication system serves user equipment (UE) identities.  Charan \emph{et al.}
make transmitter identification explicit when extending camera-based beam
prediction to multi-candidate V2I scenes
\cite{charan2023camerabasedmmwavebeam}.  Imran \emph{et al.} include transmitter
identification and object-association-based tracking in a distributed semantic
communication framework \cite{imran2024environmentsemanticcommunicationenabling}.
Nevertheless, a beam-class ranking may be accurate for a detected vehicle yet
be unusable if it is applied to another UE.  The problem is therefore not merely
to predict promising beams from a road scene.  It is to use distributed
multimodal sensing to generate target-level beam information that is associated
with, and remains applicable to, the correct communication user.

\section{Challenges}
\label{sec:introduction-challenges}

The first challenge is \emph{limited and condition-dependent target
observability}.  A single sensing modality or roadside site cannot provide
uniform coverage of a dynamic road scene.  Camera observations are constrained
by field of view, illumination, weather, and occlusion, whereas radio-sensing
point clouds may be sparse or incomplete.  Moreover, the serving BS may not
observe every road segment from which a relevant vehicle approaches.  The
central problem is therefore not the accumulation of more sensor inputs, but
the construction of a coherent target-BS-centred representation in which the
semantic information of multiview images, the geometric evidence of BS-local
ISAC sensing, and the complementary coverage of neighbouring sensing nodes can
support both vehicle detection and beam prediction under changing observation
conditions.

The second challenge is \emph{sensing-to-communication identity ambiguity}.
A perception system observes physical targets, whereas beam management is
performed for communication UEs.  These two sets are neither identical nor
naturally paired: a visible vehicle may not be an active UE, an active UE may
be temporarily unobserved, and nearby vehicles may exhibit similar spatial and
beam characteristics.  Consequently, even an accurate target-level beam
prediction is ineffective if it is applied to the wrong UE.  Communication
measurements are therefore required to establish or recover the correspondence
between UEs and perception tracks.  The correspondence must then remain valid
despite vehicle motion, short-term occlusion, missed detections, and interactions
among nearby targets.  Multi-object tracking is needed to preserve the temporal
continuity of sensing targets, while association acceptance and current-track
observation determine whether their beam information may be used.  When this
evidence is insufficient, conventional SSB-guided refinement must remain
available rather than assigning an uncertain sensing output to a communication
user.

These two challenges define the methodological scope of the thesis.  The first
concerns whether distributed multimodal sensing can produce useful target-level
beam evidence over a wider range of observation conditions; the second concerns
whether that evidence can be attributed to, and maintained for, the intended
communication UE.  Their combined communication value must subsequently be
established under a common beam-measurement and resource model, rather than
inferred from beam-prediction accuracy alone.

\section{Research Objective}
\label{sec:introduction-objective}

This thesis addresses the two methodological challenges through the proposed
Distributed Multimodal Sensing-Assisted Beam Management framework, denoted by
DMSA-BM.  The overall objective is to transform distributed multimodal
observations into target-level beam information, establish its correspondence
to the intended communication UE, and determine its value within an
NR-oriented beam-management procedure.  The first two research objectives
respond directly to target observability and identity ambiguity, respectively;
the third establishes the system-level evidence needed to evaluate their
communication consequence.

The first objective is to develop \emph{distributed multimodal BEV perception
for joint vehicle detection and beam prediction}.  Observations from cameras at
the target BS and neighbouring sensing nodes are aligned with BS-local ISAC
sensing in a common BEV representation.  Joint detection identifies the
physical targets for which beam information is available, while a Top-1 beam
classifier produces a per-target CSI beam-class posterior.  The posterior ranks
beams for subsequent association and CSI-RS candidate measurement; it is not
interpreted as a physical power distribution or used as a direct service-beam
decision.

The second objective is to develop \emph{target-to-user association with
IMM-based tracking}.  Conventional SSB and CSI-RS measurements provide
UE-indexed evidence for establishing or recovering an association with an
observed perception track.  The interacting multiple-model (IMM) multi-object
tracker maintains target continuity as vehicle motion and sensing availability
change, without treating a track identity as a communication identity.  An
association is permitted to support sensing-derived candidate selection only
when it is accepted and the corresponding track is currently observed;
otherwise, the UE follows conventional refinement.  This objective ensures that
target-level beam predictions remain applicable to the intended communication
user.

The third objective is to establish a \emph{multimodal V2I simulation and 5G
NR-oriented beam-management evaluation framework}.  The framework integrates
multimodal roadside sensing, time-varying V2I channels, beam measurement, and
resource accounting within a common system model.  It relates target detection,
beam prediction, association-conditioned sensing use, and conventional fallback
to the number of measured candidates, CSI-RS overhead, and average effective
user rate.  This objective determines whether the first two methodological
components yield a communication benefit after measurement cost has been
accounted for.

The framework is scoped to a target-BS-centred local service region.  A vehicle
entering a new serving cell establishes a new local association; no global
multi-BS identity is assumed.  Likewise, ground-truth identities and detections
are reserved for supervision, diagnosis, and upper-bound analysis; online
decisions rely exclusively on sensing outputs and communication measurements
available to the BS.  Detailed radio configurations, channel assumptions,
resource accounting, and physical-layer metrics are specified in the system
model and experimental evaluation.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:introduction-contributions}

The two methodological challenges require target-level sensing evidence to be
both observable and attributable to the correct communication user.  They also
require system-level validation to determine whether this evidence improves
beam management after measurement overhead has been included.  The principal
contributions of this thesis are therefore summarised as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item We propose a distributed multimodal BEV perception framework for
    joint vehicle detection and beam prediction.  The framework aligns camera
    observations from the target BS and neighbouring sensing nodes with a
    BS-local ISAC point cloud in a target-BS-centred BEV representation.  It
    thereby addresses the limited and condition-dependent observability of a
    single sensing site or modality, while producing per-target CSI beam-class
    posteriors for association and candidate measurement.

    \item We develop a target-to-user association framework with IMM-based
    multi-object tracking.  Conventional beam measurements establish or recover
    the association between an unbound perception track and a communication UE,
    while IMM tracking preserves the temporal continuity of the sensed target.
    Association acceptance and current-track observation restrict
    sensing-derived candidate selection to credible UE--track pairs, preventing
    target-level beam information from being applied indiscriminately across
    users and retaining SSB-guided refinement when the required evidence is
    unavailable.

    \item We establish a multimodal V2I simulation and 5G NR-oriented
    beam-management evaluation framework.  The framework combines multimodal
    sensing, time-varying V2I channels, beam measurements, downlink transmission,
    and resource accounting in a common evaluation chain.  By jointly reporting
    sensing-use and fallback behaviour, the number of measured candidates,
    CSI-RS overhead, and average effective user rate, it distinguishes
    target-level beam-prediction performance from a communication benefit under
    the stated beam-management procedure.
\end{enumerate}

Together, these contributions connect distributed perception, user-specific
association, and system-level evaluation without treating sensing as a
replacement for communication measurement.  Quantitative comparisons are
reported under the stated common experimental protocol and evidence boundaries.

\section{Thesis Organization}
\label{sec:introduction-organization}

The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows.  Chapter~\ref{ch:background}
reviews beam alignment in high-mobility V2I networks, sensing-assisted beam
prediction, distributed cooperative perception in V2X networks, and
target-to-user association in multi-candidate scenarios.  It uses the latter
topic to connect temporal tracking and association-aware beam-management
evaluation, thereby establishing the related-work basis for user-specific beam
alignment.

Chapter~\ref{ch:system-model} establishes the local V2I network architecture,
the distributed multimodal sensing model, and the multi-user downlink model.  It
then relates CSI-RS acquisition and feedback, RZF precoding, beam-management
overhead, and effective rate, establishing the communication interface through
which target-level evidence affects VUE-level performance.
Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} presents DMSA-BM, including distributed multimodal
BEV perception, joint vehicle detection and beam prediction, IMM-based
multi-object tracking, measurement-assisted target-to-user association, and
association-acceptance-gated CSI-RS candidate selection.

Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments} describes the multimodal V2I dataset, experimental
setup, evaluation methodology, and communication baselines.  It reports
perception and beam-prediction performance, reliability-gated beam-management
performance, and tracking performance, and relates DMSA-BM's sensing-use and
fallback behaviour to CSI-RS overhead and average effective user rate under a
common radio configuration.  Finally, Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} summarises the findings
supported by the evaluation, discusses the limitations of the local BS scope and
communication abstraction, and outlines extensions towards cross-BS association,
measurement calibration, and more detailed protocol-level assessment.
