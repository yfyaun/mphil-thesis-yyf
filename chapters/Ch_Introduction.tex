\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:introduction-background}

Vehicular networks are expected to support connected mobility and intelligent
transportation services whose data-rate and latency requirements are difficult
to meet with conventional wide-beam links.  Millimetre-wave and other
high-frequency bands offer the required bandwidth, but their gains rely on
narrow directional beams.  In vehicle-to-infrastructure (V2I) communication, a
base station (BS) must therefore maintain beam alignment while the relative
geometry and propagation conditions of a vehicle change rapidly.  Vision-aided
V2X beam alignment has, for example, used visual vehicle information to infer a
beam pair and predict its coherence time \cite{xu2022computervisionaidedmmwave}.
Beam alignment is consequently a recurrent decision problem rather than a
one-off access operation.

Current high-frequency systems obtain direct communication evidence through
synchronisation signal block (SSB) sweeps, channel-state-information
reference-signal (CSI-RS) measurements, and subsequent channel-state-information
acquisition and refinement.  These procedures are indispensable at initial
access and whenever the existing link is uncertain.  Their cost, however,
increases with the beam-search space and the frequency of remeasurement.  In a
dynamic road scene, a measurement can become stale before data transmission;
repeated exhaustive scans reduce that uncertainty at the expense of
time--frequency resources that could otherwise carry payload data.  The central
trade-off is therefore between measurement reliability and measurement cost,
rather than between alternative beam-index classifiers alone.

Environmental sensing offers a route to narrowing the measurement search
space.  Alrabeiah \emph{et al.} use BS-mounted cameras and sub-6~GHz side
information to predict mmWave beams and blockage events
\cite{alrabeiah2019millimeterwavebasestations}.  Demirhan and Alkhateeb use
radar observations to infer mmWave beams and examine the potential reduction in
beam-training overhead \cite{demirhan2021radaraided6gbeam}.  Jiang \emph{et al.}
use LiDAR observations to predict current and future beams in real-world V2I
links \cite{jiang2022lidaraidedfuturebeam}.  These studies demonstrate that
physical-scene observations can guide which communication measurements deserve
priority.

For roadside V2I systems, the sensing opportunity is inherently spatially
distributed.  The serving BS may observe the local road region through multiview
cameras and a BS-local ISAC point cloud, while neighbouring sensing nodes (SNs)
can reveal road segments that are distant, weakly visible, or occluded from the
serving BS.  A target-BS-centered bird's-eye-view (BEV) representation provides
a common local frame in which these observations can be aligned.
Lift-Splat-Shoot demonstrates how multi-camera features can be projected to a
BEV grid \cite{philion2020liftsplatshootencoding}.  BEVFusion shows how camera
and LiDAR features can be fused in a unified BEV representation
\cite{liu2024bevfusionmultitaskmultisensorfusion}.  V2X-ViT further demonstrates
the value of fusing observations from multiple V2X agents while accounting for
asynchrony and pose error \cite{xu2022v2xvitvehicletoeverythingcooperativeperception}.
In the present setting, a local BEV representation is used to produce
vehicle-level evidence for both detection and beam prediction.

This sensing opportunity also exposes a distinction that is often hidden in
beam-prediction formulations.  Sensing observes physical vehicles, whereas the
communication system serves user equipment (UE) identities.  Charan \emph{et al.}
make transmitter identification explicit when extending camera-based beam
prediction to multi-candidate V2I scenes
\cite{charan2023camerabasedmmwavebeam}.  Imran \emph{et al.} include transmitter
identification and object-association-based tracking in a distributed semantic
communication framework \cite{imran2024environmentsemanticcommunicationenabling}.
Nevertheless, a beam-quality ranking may be accurate for a detected vehicle yet
be unusable if it is applied to another UE.  The problem is therefore not merely
to predict promising beams from a road scene.  It is to use distributed
multimodal sensing to generate beam hints that are associated with, and remain
applicable to, the correct communication user.

\section{Challenges}
\label{sec:introduction-challenges}

The first challenge is \emph{condition-aware distributed multimodal
perception}.  A single sensing modality or sensing site has unavoidable blind
spots.  Camera observations are affected by field of view, illumination,
weather, and occlusion; point-cloud observations can be sparse or incomplete;
and the serving BS may not observe all relevant road segments.  The technical
issue is therefore not simply to aggregate additional inputs.  It is to align
semantic information from multiview images, geometric information from ISAC
sensing, and complementary coverage from neighbouring SNs in a target-BS local
representation.  That representation must remain informative for vehicle
detection and conditional beam-quality ranking across variations in visibility,
blockage, traffic configuration, and weather.

The second challenge is \emph{target-to-user association and temporal
maintenance}.  In an operational multi-vehicle V2I scene, a perception module
returns a set of physical targets, whereas SSB and CSI-RS measurements are
indexed by UEs.  A visible vehicle need not be an active UE, an active UE can
be temporarily unobserved, and nearby vehicles can have similar spatial or beam
characteristics.  Hence, a correct beam ranking assigned to the wrong UE still
produces an incorrect beam-alignment action.  Communication measurements must
therefore anchor the initial association between a perception track and a UE.
That association can subsequently be weakened by mobility, short-term
occlusion, missed detection, or close interactions among vehicles.  Multi-object
state estimation and gated data association must maintain its temporal validity
and trigger measurement-assisted re-association when the available evidence is
no longer credible.  Tracking thus maintains a user association; it is not a
visual mechanism for inferring UE identity.

The third challenge is \emph{association-aware candidate reduction and
evaluation}.  Sensing should not replace communication measurement whenever a
predicted Top-$K$ list is available.  A candidate set can be misleading when
the current target is poorly observed, the target-to-user association is
ambiguous, or the predicted beam distribution is diffuse.  A valid
sensing-assisted policy must use a beam hint only under credible association
and current sensing evidence, and must otherwise fall back to conventional
refinement.  Its merit must be assessed jointly through candidate recall,
CSI-RS overhead, effective rate, lower-tail rate, and outage.  This evaluation
must expose how an incorrect association, re-association, or fallback event
propagates to the final user outcome; Top-$K$ accuracy alone cannot establish a
communication benefit.

Together, these challenges define the scope of this thesis: distributed
multimodal perception provides target-level beam evidence, target-to-user
association determines its user-level applicability, and association-aware
evaluation determines whether its use is worthwhile under a common resource
model.

\section{Research Objective}
\label{sec:introduction-objective}

This thesis addresses the foregoing challenges through an integrated,
BS-centric sensing-to-communication framework.  Its objective is to transform
distributed multimodal observations into beam-management information that is
useful for the correct communication UE, and to determine the communication
value of that information under a common measurement model.  The framework is
organised around three connected objectives, which directly motivate the three
contributions of this thesis.

The first objective is to establish \emph{distributed multimodal BEV perception
for joint vehicle detection and beam prediction}.  The target BS combines its
local sensing with complementary observations from neighbouring SNs in a common
BEV representation.  This objective addresses the limited observability of a
single sensing site or modality: vehicle detection identifies relevant physical
targets, while beam prediction estimates the distribution of beam quality for
each target.  The resulting distribution ranks candidates for communication
measurement; it does not replace communication measurement.

The second objective is to realise \emph{target-to-user association with
IMM-based tracking}.  It addresses the fact that a physical sensing target and
a communication UE are distinct entities whose relationship cannot be assumed
in a multi-vehicle scene.  Communication-side measurements establish or recover
the association, while IMM-based tracking maintains its validity as vehicles
move and observations evolve.  The purpose is to preserve the applicability of
a beam hint to its intended UE, not to infer communication identity from
perception alone.

The third objective is to construct a \emph{Multimodal V2I Simulator and 5G
NR-Oriented Beam-Management Evaluation Framework}.  Built on the available
multimodal V2I data assets, this system-level environment compares candidate
measurement and conventional fallback under a common measurement and resource
model.  It evaluates how perception and association quality affect measurement
overhead, link reliability, and communication performance, rather than equating
a beam-prediction accuracy metric with an end-to-end system gain.

The framework is scoped to a target-BS-centered local service region.  A vehicle
entering a new serving cell establishes a new local association; no global
multi-BS identity is assumed.  Likewise, ground-truth identities and detections
are reserved for diagnostic or upper-bound analyses, whereas the formal decision
path uses sensing and communication information available to the BS.  Detailed
radio configurations, channel assumptions, scheduling rules, and physical-layer
metrics are specified in the system model and experimental evaluation.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:introduction-contributions}

The foregoing challenges require an integrated sensing-to-communication design,
rather than a collection of independent prediction modules.  Accordingly, this
thesis contributes three mutually reinforcing research components for
user-specific beam-alignment decisions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Distributed Multimodal BEV Perception for Joint Vehicle
    Detection and Beam Prediction.} A target-BS-centered BEV perception model
    jointly detects vehicles and estimates a per-target CSI beam-quality
    distribution from multiview cameras at the target BS and neighbouring SNs,
    together with a BS-local ISAC point cloud.  The model provides target-level
    beam evidence that can rank candidate measurements under complementary
    sensing conditions.

    \item \textbf{Target-to-User Association with IMM-Based Tracking.} A
    measurement-anchored association mechanism connects unbound perception
    targets with communication UEs.  Conventional measurements establish or
    recover the association, while an interacting multiple-model (IMM) tracker
    maintains it as targets evolve over time.  Reliability assessment determines
    whether a sensing-derived beam hint can be applied to a UE or whether the
    procedure should revert to conventional refinement.

    \item \textbf{Multimodal V2I Simulator and 5G NR-Oriented Beam-Management
    Evaluation Framework.} A system-level simulator is established on existing
    multimodal V2I data assets to evaluate sensing-assisted policies under a
    common 5G NR-oriented measurement and resource model.  It connects
    perception and association quality to candidate recall, CSI-RS overhead,
    effective rate, lower-tail rate, and outage, thereby distinguishing a useful
    target-level beam ranking from a user-specific beam-management benefit.
\end{enumerate}

The contributions are stated at the formulation, method, and evaluation levels.
Quantitative comparative claims are restricted to results obtained under the
corresponding frozen protocol and held-out evaluation.

\section{Thesis Organization}
\label{sec:introduction-organization}

The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows.  Chapter~\ref{ch:background}
reviews beam alignment in high-mobility V2I networks, sensing-assisted beam
prediction, distributed cooperative perception in V2X networks, and
target-to-user association in multi-candidate scenarios.  It uses the latter
topic to connect temporal tracking and association-aware beam-management
evaluation, thereby establishing the related-work basis for user-specific beam
alignment.

Chapter~\ref{ch:system-model} establishes the local V2I network architecture,
the distributed multimodal sensing model, and the NR-oriented communication and
beam-management procedure.  It then formulates the sensing, target-to-user
association, and rate--resource objectives that connect target-level evidence to
VUE-level performance.  Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} presents the proposed
framework, including distributed multimodal BEV perception, joint vehicle
detection and beam prediction, target-to-user association with IMM-based
tracking, reliability-aware candidate scanning, and the communication interface.

Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments} describes the data protocol, comparison methods,
layered metrics, and the Multimodal V2I Simulator with its 5G NR-oriented
beam-management evaluation framework.  It examines how distributed multimodal
perception, target-to-user association with IMM-based tracking, and candidate
measurement decisions affect communication performance under common resource
assumptions.  Finally, Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} summarises the findings
supported by the evaluation, discusses the limitations of the local BS scope and
communication abstraction, and outlines extensions towards cross-BS association,
measurement calibration, and more detailed protocol-level assessment.
