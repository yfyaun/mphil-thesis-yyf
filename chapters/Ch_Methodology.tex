\chapter{Proposed Sensing-Assisted Beam Management Framework}
\label{ch:methodology}

This chapter specifies the proposed sensing-assisted beam-management framework
under the system model of Chapter~\ref{ch:system-model}.  The framework is
organised around a strict separation between a physical vehicle observed by the
sensing system and a VUE addressed by the communication system.  Multimodal
sensing first produces target-level evidence in a target-BS-centred BEV
representation.  Conventional communication measurements then establish the
target-to-user association required to use that evidence, and CSI-RS candidate
reduction is permitted only while the association is accepted and its track is
supported by a current observation.  Sensing therefore ranks measurements; it
does not replace CSI-RS-based service-beam selection.

The proposed method couples perception, tracking, association, and candidate
selection.  Distributed multimodal BEV perception produces target detections
and beam predictions, while IMM-based multi-object tracking retains temporally
consistent evidence for physical vehicles.  Measurement-assisted target-to-user
association and association-gated candidate selection then connect this evidence
to an NR-oriented beam-management procedure through conventional beam
measurements.  Chapter~\ref{ch:system-model} defines the associated resource and
effective-rate models, and Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments} presents the system-level
evaluation.

\section{Framework Architecture}
\label{sec:method-framework}

For each target BS \(b\) and sensing epoch \(n\), the framework follows four
ordered stages.  The first two stages run on the sensing time line and produce
temporally coherent evidence for physical vehicles.  The remaining stages are
invoked when this evidence must support a VUE-specific measurement decision.
This ordering makes explicit how a sensing target without a communication
identity can assist beam management without being treated as an already
identified VUE.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \emph{Distributed multimodal perception and beam
    prediction.}  The network processes \(\mathcal{O}_{b,n}\), defined in
    Section~\ref{sec:system-sensing}, in a target-BS-centred BEV frame.  It
    outputs a set of vehicle detections without communication identities,
    \(\{(d_{b,i,n},\mathbf{p}_{b,i,n})\}_i\).  Each \(d_{b,i,n}\) contains a
    BEV position, available attributes, and detection confidence, while
    \(\mathbf{p}_{b,i,n}\) is a 192-way beam-class posterior.  Its ranked
    entries provide candidate scores for that physical target; they are neither
    a VUE identifier nor a service-beam decision.

    \item \emph{IMM-based multi-object tracking.}  The CV and CT branches of
    the IMM predict the motion of each physical target and update their model
    probabilities from the current BEV detection.  Innovation gating and
    one-to-one detection--track assignment associate current observations with
    predicted tracks.  The resulting track set \(\mathcal{R}_{b,n}\) maintains
    target position, motion state, uncertainty, confidence, observation
    history, and the current beam-class posterior.  It establishes sensing
    continuity only.  A perception track remains distinct from a communication
    UE.

    \item \emph{Initial or renewed target-to-user association.}  At initial
    access, after a serving-cell change, or when an existing UE--track
    association loses support, conventional SSB and CSI-RS measurements provide
    VUE-indexed beam
    evidence.  The measured CSI-RS beam of each affected VUE is evaluated under
    the beam-class posteriors of currently observed tracks.  SSB-parent
    consistency first excludes infeasible pairs; a one-to-one assignment with
    an unmatched alternative then determines whether the measurement evidence
    supports an accepted UE--track mapping \(\pi_{b,n}\).  Ambiguous pairs
    remain without an accepted association rather than being forced into an
    incorrect UE--track assignment.

    \item \emph{Association-gated candidate selection.}  An accepted mapping
    is used only while its associated track is currently observed.  The
    corresponding beam-class posterior then defines a fixed or adaptive CSI-RS
    candidate set.  If no association is accepted or the associated track is
    absent from the current observation, conventional refinement is retained.
    In either case, the service beam is chosen only after CSI-RS quality has
    been measured over the selected candidate set.
\end{enumerate}

The resulting information flow is concisely represented by
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{O}_{b,n}
    \longrightarrow
    \bigl\{d_{b,i,n},\mathbf{p}_{b,i,n}\bigr\}_{i}
    \longrightarrow
    \mathcal{R}_{b,n}
    \longrightarrow
    \pi_{b,n}
    \longrightarrow
    \mathcal{C}_{b,u,n},
    \label{eq:method-decision-path}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{R}_{b,n}\) is the set of perception tracks,
\(\pi_{b,n}\) denotes the partial mapping formed from accepted UE--track
associations, and \(\mathcal{C}_{b,u,n}\) is either a sensing-derived CSI-RS
candidate set or a conventional refinement set.  This sequence defines the
interfaces among the perception, tracking, association, and beam-measurement
models developed below.

Four information boundaries define the decision path.  First, the perception
network receives calibrated camera and point-cloud observations only; link
coordinates, VUE identities, and oracle beam labels are excluded from its
forward input.  Second, the IMM propagates BEV target states and uncertainty,
not communication identities.  Third, initial or renewed association uses
conventional SSB/CSI-RS evidence together with predicted beam-class posterior
scores, without ground-truth IDs or a geometric inversion from position to beam
index.
Finally, a sensing prediction may reduce CSI-RS measurements only for an
accepted association whose track is currently observed; the measured CSI-RS quality, rather
than the neural prediction, selects the service beam.  These boundaries preserve
the distinct functions of perception, tracking, association, and communication
measurement while excluding oracle information from online decisions.

\section{Distributed Multimodal BEV Perception and Beam Prediction}
\label{sec:method-perception-learning}

This section formulates the distributed multimodal perception and beam-prediction
model.  It first constructs a target-BS-centred BEV representation from
distributed visual observations and BS-local ISAC sensing,
then decodes vehicle-level detections and beam-class scores from the shared
representation.  The final subsection defines the Top-1 classification
supervision used to learn beam prediction without treating the prediction as a
direct service-beam declaration.

\subsection{Multimodal BEV Representation}
\label{subsec:method-bev-representation}

The BEV representation provides a common spatial interface for distributed
visual observations, BS-local ISAC sensing, vehicle detection, and beam
prediction.  Let \(\mathcal{J}_b=\{b\}\cup\mathcal{N}_b\) denote the target BS
and its neighbouring SNs.  For each node \(j\in\mathcal{J}_b\), calibrated
multiview images are encoded and lifted into the BEV frame of BS \(b\) as follows.
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{cam}}_{j\rightarrow b,n}
    =
    \Phi_{\mathrm{img}}\!
    \left(
    \mathcal{I}_{j,n};\mathbf{T}_{j\rightarrow b}
    \right),
    \label{eq:method-camera-lift}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{T}_{j\rightarrow b}\) denotes the calibration information that
expresses node \(j\)'s visual evidence in the target-BS-centred coordinate
system.  All node features are transformed to this common BEV frame before
cross-node aggregation.

The distributed visual evidence is aggregated as
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{cam}}_{b,n}
    =
    \Phi_{\mathrm{node}}
    \left(
    \left\{
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{cam}}_{j\rightarrow b,n},
    \mathbf{m}^{\mathrm{cam}}_{j,n}
    \right\}_{j\in\mathcal{J}_b}
    \right),
    \label{eq:method-node-aggregation}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{m}^{\mathrm{cam}}_{j,n}\) captures view availability or
reliability.  The study compares node aggregation mechanisms, including uniform
aggregation and learned cross-node attention, within the common operator
\(\Phi_{\mathrm{node}}\).  Across these mechanisms, neighbouring nodes serve the
same algorithmic purpose.  They supplement the target-BS view when its coverage
is incomplete or affected by occlusion.

The BS-local ISAC point cloud is independently encoded on the same grid,
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{isac}}_{b,n}
    =
    \Phi_{\mathrm{pc}}\!
    \left(\mathcal{P}_{b,n}\right).
    \label{eq:method-isac-encoding}
\end{equation}
The camera and point-cloud representations are combined by a modality-fusion
operator,
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}_{b,n}
    =
    \Phi_{\mathrm{modal}}
    \left(
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{cam}}_{b,n},
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{isac}}_{b,n};
    \mathbf{m}^{\mathrm{modal}}_{b,n}
    \right),
    \label{eq:method-modal-fusion}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{m}^{\mathrm{modal}}_{b,n}\) represents modality availability or
confidence.  Modality gating can downweight unreliable evidence, while node
aggregation preserves complementary visual observations.  Neither operation is
used to infer a communication identity; their role is to construct a
target-level scene representation with improved observability.

The shared BEV representation aligns a detected vehicle and its beam-prediction
supervision with the local service region of BS \(b\).  It also enables
cross-view evidence to influence both detection and beam prediction
before target-level outputs are decoded.  The resulting feature map
\(\mathbf{F}_{b,n}\) is therefore shared by the two prediction tasks described
next.

\subsection{Joint Detection and Beam Prediction}
\label{subsec:method-target-prediction}

The joint prediction head maps \(\mathbf{F}_{b,n}\) to a vehicle-centre
heatmap, target attributes, and spatially dense CSI beam-class logits as follows.
\begin{equation}
    \left(
    \mathbf{H}_{b,n},
    \mathbf{A}_{b,n},
    \mathbf{P}_{b,n}
    \right)
    =
    \Phi_{\mathrm{joint}}\!
    \left(\mathbf{F}_{b,n}\right),
    \label{eq:method-joint-head}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{H}_{b,n}\) is the centre heatmap, \(\mathbf{A}_{b,n}\)
contains geometric or motion-related attributes, and
\(\mathbf{P}_{b,n}\) contains \(M_{\mathrm{CSI}}=192\) class logits at every BEV
location.  Local maxima of \(\mathbf{H}_{b,n}\) define the vehicle centres.  At
the \(i\)-th decoded centre, the target-level output is
\begin{equation}
    d_{b,i,n}
    =
    \left(
    \widehat{\mathbf{r}}_{b,i,n},
    \widehat{\mathbf{a}}_{b,i,n},
    \widehat{c}_{b,i,n}
    \right),\qquad
    \mathbf{p}_{b,i,n}
    =
    \operatorname{softmax}\!\left(
    \operatorname{Sample}
    \left(
    \mathbf{P}_{b,n},
    \widehat{\mathbf{r}}_{b,i,n}
    \right)
    \right).
    \label{eq:method-target-output}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\widehat{\mathbf{r}}_{b,i,n}\) is the estimated BEV centre,
\(\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_{b,i,n}\) contains available size, heading, and velocity
attributes, and \(\widehat{c}_{b,i,n}\) is the detection confidence.  Sampling
\(\mathbf{P}_{b,n}\) at the decoded centre converts the dense logits into a
beam-class posterior specific to the corresponding physical target.
Consequently, several simultaneously visible vehicles can receive distinct
candidate rankings from a single shared BEV representation.

During training, the BS--UE link coordinate identifies the BEV location at
which beam supervision is sampled.  It acts solely as a supervision index.  It
is excluded from the forward model and is not inverted to obtain a beam index at
inference time.  The prediction head also receives neither the VUE identity nor
an oracle track identity.  Equation~\eqref{eq:method-target-output} therefore
defines a target-indexed output without a communication identity, which remains
suitable for the subsequent association stage.

\subsection{Top-1 Beam Classification}
\label{subsec:method-top1-beam-classification}

For a supervised BS--UE link, let \(g_{b,u,n}(k)\) be the physical CSI beam gain
in Equation~\eqref{eq:system-beam-gain}.  The beam-prediction label
is the strongest CSI beam,
\begin{equation}
    k^\star_{b,u,n}
    =
    \underset{k\in\{1,\ldots,M_{\mathrm{CSI}}\}}{\arg\max}
    g_{b,u,n}(k).
    \label{eq:method-top1-beam-label}
\end{equation}
At the corresponding supervised BEV location, let \(\mathbf{z}_{b,i,n}\) be
the predicted logits and
\(\mathbf{p}_{b,i,n}=\operatorname{softmax}(\mathbf{z}_{b,i,n})\) the
beam-class posterior.  The beam-prediction loss is the standard Top-1
cross-entropy objective,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{beam}}
    =
    -\log p_{b,i,n}\!\left(k^\star_{b,u,n}\right).
    \label{eq:method-beam-loss}
\end{equation}
This supervision trains the network to identify the strongest codebook beam for
each target-level sample.  The full posterior remains available at inference to
order candidate beams, but it is not interpreted as a prediction of the
physical CSI power distribution.

The complete training objective combines the detection and beam-classification
terms,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{joint}}
    =
    \lambda_{\mathrm{hm}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{hm}}
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{attr}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{attr}}
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{beam}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{beam}},
    \label{eq:method-joint-loss}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{hm}}\) and
\(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{attr}}\) supervise the vehicle-centre and target-attribute
outputs, respectively.  The training target is a beam class rather than a
soft CSI-power distribution.  Candidate recall and the physical power retained
by a predicted Top-\(K\) list characterise the resulting beam ranking but do not
enter \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{joint}}\).  The network exports scores for all
\(M_{\mathrm{CSI}}\) beams, whereas \(K_{\mathrm{scan}}\) alone specifies the
number of CSI-RS beams actually measured after a candidate-use decision.

\section{IMM-Based Multi-Object Tracking}
\label{sec:method-imm-tracking}

The perception network produces a detection without a communication identity
whenever a vehicle is visible.  Sensing-derived beam information can be applied
over time only if the physical target is maintained consistently as vehicles
move, turn, or are intermittently observed.  This section therefore estimates
the state of physical targets only.  Each target BS maintains an independent
local track set; a track is neither a recognised VUE nor a cross-cell identity.
The interacting multiple model (IMM) filter
combines a constant-velocity (CV) hypothesis with a coordinated-turn (CT)
hypothesis, allowing the motion estimate to adapt to the local vehicle
trajectory.

\subsection{Motion Models}
\label{subsec:method-motion-models}

For the CV model, the state of track \(r\) is represented in Cartesian BEV
coordinates as
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{CV}}_{r,n}
    =
    [x_{r,n},y_{r,n},v_{x,r,n},v_{y,r,n}]^{\mathsf T}.
    \label{eq:method-cv-state}
\end{equation}
With sensing interval \(\Delta t\), its linear process and position-measurement
models are
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{CV}}_{r,n}
    =
    \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{CV}}\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{CV}}_{r,n-1}
    +\mathbf{w}^{\mathrm{CV}}_{r,n},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{z}_{i,n}
    =
    \mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{CV}}\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{CV}}_{r,n}
    +\mathbf{v}_{i,n},
    \label{eq:method-cv-model}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{CV}}
    =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1&0&\Delta t&0\\
        0&1&0&\Delta t\\
        0&0&1&0\\
        0&0&0&1
    \end{bmatrix},
    \qquad
    \mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{CV}}
    =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1&0&0&0\\
        0&1&0&0
    \end{bmatrix}.
    \label{eq:method-cv-matrices}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\mathbf{z}_{i,n}=[\widehat{x}_{b,i,n},\widehat{y}_{b,i,n}]^{\mathsf T}\)
is the BEV centre of detection \(d_{b,i,n}\), and
\(\mathbf{w}^{\mathrm{CV}}_{r,n}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{Q}_{\mathrm{CV}})\)
and \(\mathbf{v}_{i,n}\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{R}_{i,n})\) represent
process and detection uncertainty.  The CV model is appropriate when target
motion is locally close to rectilinear.  In particular, the observation model
is defined by the BEV detection interface of the present framework; it is not
identified with the measurement model of a radar- or communication-based
tracker.

The CT model uses the speed--heading representation
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{CT}}_{r,n}
    =
    [x_{r,n},y_{r,n},v_{r,n},\psi_{r,n},\omega_{r,n}]^{\mathsf T},
    \label{eq:method-ct-state}
\end{equation}
where \(v_{r,n}\), \(\psi_{r,n}\), and \(\omega_{r,n}\) denote speed, heading,
and turn rate.  For \(|\omega_{r,n-1}|>0\), the coordinated-turn state evolution
is
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{CT}}_{r,n}
    =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        x_{r,n-1}+\dfrac{v_{r,n-1}}{\omega_{r,n-1}}
        \left[\sin(\psi_{r,n-1}+\omega_{r,n-1}\Delta t)-\sin\psi_{r,n-1}\right]\\[3pt]
        y_{r,n-1}+\dfrac{v_{r,n-1}}{\omega_{r,n-1}}
        \left[\cos\psi_{r,n-1}-\cos(\psi_{r,n-1}+\omega_{r,n-1}\Delta t)\right]\\[3pt]
        v_{r,n-1}\\
        \psi_{r,n-1}+\omega_{r,n-1}\Delta t\\
        \omega_{r,n-1}
    \end{bmatrix}
    +\mathbf{w}^{\mathrm{CT}}_{r,n}
    \triangleq
    f_{\mathrm{CT}}\!\left(\mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{CT}}_{r,n-1}\right)
    +\mathbf{w}^{\mathrm{CT}}_{r,n}.
    \label{eq:method-ct-model}
\end{equation}
The zero-turn-rate limit gives
\(x_{r,n}=x_{r,n-1}+v_{r,n-1}\Delta t\cos\psi_{r,n-1}\) and
\(y_{r,n}=y_{r,n-1}+v_{r,n-1}\Delta t\sin\psi_{r,n-1}\), recovering local
straight-line motion without a numerical singularity.  The CT measurement
function extracts position, \(h_{\mathrm{CT}}(\mathbf{x})=[x,y]^{\mathsf T}\),
and its EKF transition and measurement matrices are the corresponding Jacobians
\(\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{CT}}=\partial f_{\mathrm{CT}}/\partial\mathbf{x}\) and
\(\mathbf{H}_{\mathrm{CT}}=\partial h_{\mathrm{CT}}/\partial\mathbf{x}\),
evaluated at the predicted state.  Thus, the CT kinematics describe vehicle
motion, whereas the update remains conditioned on the position observations
and effective detection uncertainty produced by the multimodal BEV detector.
Figure~\ref{fig:method-ct-model} depicts the speed--heading and turn-rate
parameterisation used by the CT branch.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.66\textwidth]{figures/ct_model.pdf}
    \caption{Coordinated-turn motion model in the local BEV plane.  The state
    comprises position, speed, heading, and turn rate; the latter determines
    the local turning radius under nonzero angular velocity.}
    \label{fig:method-ct-model}
\end{figure}

The two model states use different coordinates.  Let
\(\mathcal{T}_{q\rightarrow \ell}(\cdot)\) denote the deterministic conversion
between their Cartesian and speed--heading forms, and let
\(\mathbf{J}_{q\rightarrow \ell}\) be its Jacobian.  These transformations are
applied whenever states or covariances are exchanged between models, allowing
the IMM to retain a consistent uncertainty representation.
In particular, the state components are converted according to
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{CV}\rightarrow\mathrm{CT}}
    \left([x,y,v_x,v_y]^{\mathsf T}\right)
    =
    [x,y,\sqrt{v_x^2+v_y^2},\operatorname{atan2}(v_y,v_x),0]^{\mathsf T},
    \label{eq:method-cv-to-ct}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{CT}\rightarrow\mathrm{CV}}
    \left([x,y,v,\psi,\omega]^{\mathsf T}\right)
    =
    [x,y,v\cos\psi,v\sin\psi]^{\mathsf T}.
    \label{eq:method-ct-to-cv}
\end{equation}
The corresponding Jacobians propagate covariance through these transformations;
the zero turn-rate component in the CV-to-CT conversion expresses the absence
of turn-rate information in the CV state rather than a permanent motion
constraint.

\subsection{IMM Filtering and Track Management}
\label{subsec:method-imm-filtering}

Figure~\ref{fig:method-imm-flow} summarises the IMM recursion and its
connection to detection-to-track management.  Each model first receives a
mixed state, then performs its own prediction and measurement update; the
innovation likelihood updates the mode probabilities before the branch states
are fused.  The equations below formalise this sequence.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figures/diagram_imm.pdf}
    \caption{IMM filtering and track-management flow.  CV and CT hypotheses
    are mixed, propagated, and corrected using the current detection
    observation; their likelihood-weighted estimates are then fused to update
    the physical-target track.}
    \label{fig:method-imm-flow}
\end{figure}

Let \(\mathcal{Q}=\{\mathrm{CV},\mathrm{CT}\}\) be the set of motion models and
\(\pi_{q\ell}=\Pr(m_n=\ell\mid m_{n-1}=q)\) the Markov transition probability
from model \(q\) to model \(\ell\).  Given posterior mode probabilities
\(\mu^{(q)}_{r,n-1}\), the predicted probability and mixing probability for
model \(\ell\) are
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\mu}^{(\ell)}_{r,n}
    =
    \sum_{q\in\mathcal{Q}}\pi_{q\ell}\mu^{(q)}_{r,n-1},
    \qquad
    \mu^{(q\mid\ell)}_{r,n-1}
    =
    \frac{\pi_{q\ell}\mu^{(q)}_{r,n-1}}
    {\bar{\mu}^{(\ell)}_{r,n}}.
    \label{eq:method-imm-mixing-probability}
\end{equation}
The state and covariance supplied to model \(\ell\) are consequently
\begin{align}
    \widehat{\mathbf{x}}^{(\ell)}_{r,0,n-1}
    &=
    \sum_{q\in\mathcal{Q}}
    \mu^{(q\mid\ell)}_{r,n-1}
    \mathcal{T}_{q\rightarrow\ell}
    \left(\widehat{\mathbf{x}}^{(q)}_{r,n-1}\right),
    \label{eq:method-imm-mixed-state}\\
    \mathbf{P}^{(\ell)}_{r,0,n-1}
    &=
    \sum_{q\in\mathcal{Q}}
    \mu^{(q\mid\ell)}_{r,n-1}
    \left[
    \mathbf{J}_{q\rightarrow\ell}\mathbf{P}^{(q)}_{r,n-1}
    \mathbf{J}_{q\rightarrow\ell}^{\mathsf T}
    +\mathbf{e}^{(q,\ell)}_{r,n-1}
    \left(\mathbf{e}^{(q,\ell)}_{r,n-1}\right)^{\mathsf T}
    \right],
    \label{eq:method-imm-mixed-covariance}
\end{align}
where \(\mathbf{e}^{(q,\ell)}_{r,n-1}\) is the difference between the
transformed state of mode \(q\) and the mixed state of mode \(\ell\).

Each mixed state is propagated and corrected by its model-conditioned filter.
With \(f_{\ell}\) denoting either the CV or CT transition, the filtering
recursion is
\begin{align}
    \widehat{\mathbf{x}}^{(\ell)-}_{r,n}
    &=f_{\ell}\!\left(\widehat{\mathbf{x}}^{(\ell)}_{r,0,n-1}\right),
    \label{eq:method-imm-prediction}\\
    \mathbf{P}^{(\ell)-}_{r,n}
    &=\mathbf{F}_{\ell}\mathbf{P}^{(\ell)}_{r,0,n-1}
    \mathbf{F}_{\ell}^{\mathsf T}+\mathbf{Q}_{\ell},\\
    \boldsymbol{\nu}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}
    &=\mathbf{z}_{i,n}-h_{\ell}\!\left(\widehat{\mathbf{x}}^{(\ell)-}_{r,n}\right),
    \label{eq:method-imm-innovation}\\
    \mathbf{S}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}
    &=\mathbf{H}_{\ell}\mathbf{P}^{(\ell)-}_{r,n}
    \mathbf{H}_{\ell}^{\mathsf T}+\mathbf{R}_{i,n},\\
    \mathbf{K}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}
    &=\mathbf{P}^{(\ell)-}_{r,n}\mathbf{H}_{\ell}^{\mathsf T}
    \left(\mathbf{S}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}\right)^{-1},\\
    \widehat{\mathbf{x}}^{(\ell)}_{r,n}
    &=\widehat{\mathbf{x}}^{(\ell)-}_{r,n}
    +\mathbf{K}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}\boldsymbol{\nu}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n},
    \label{eq:method-imm-state-update}\\
    \mathbf{P}^{(\ell)}_{r,n}
    &=(\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{K}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}\mathbf{H}_{\ell})
    \mathbf{P}^{(\ell)-}_{r,n}
    (\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{K}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}\mathbf{H}_{\ell})^{\mathsf T}
    +\mathbf{K}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}\mathbf{R}_{i,n}
    \left(\mathbf{K}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}\right)^{\mathsf T}.
    \label{eq:method-imm-covariance-update}
\end{align}
For the CV branch, \(\mathbf{F}_{\ell}\) and \(\mathbf{H}_{\ell}\) are the
linear matrices in Equation~\eqref{eq:method-cv-matrices}; for the CT branch,
they are the EKF Jacobians.  The innovation likelihood updates the model weight as follows.
\begin{equation}
    \Lambda^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}
    =
    \frac{\exp\!\left[-\tfrac{1}{2}
    \boldsymbol{\nu}^{(\ell)\mathsf T}_{r,i,n}
    \left(\mathbf{S}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}\right)^{-1}
    \boldsymbol{\nu}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}\right]}
    {\sqrt{(2\pi)^2\left|\mathbf{S}^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}\right|}},
    \qquad
    \mu^{(\ell)}_{r,n}
    =
    \frac{\Lambda^{(\ell)}_{r,i,n}\bar{\mu}^{(\ell)}_{r,n}}
    {\sum_{j\in\mathcal{Q}}
    \Lambda^{(j)}_{r,i,n}\bar{\mu}^{(j)}_{r,n}}.
    \label{eq:method-imm-mode-update}
\end{equation}
Let \(\mathcal{T}_{\ell\rightarrow\mathrm{c}}\) map a mode-conditioned estimate
to a common output coordinate.  The IMM estimate is obtained by moment matching as follows.
\begin{align}
    \widehat{\mathbf{x}}_{r,n}
    &=
    \sum_{\ell\in\mathcal{Q}}
    \mu^{(\ell)}_{r,n}
    \mathcal{T}_{\ell\rightarrow\mathrm{c}}
    \left(\widehat{\mathbf{x}}^{(\ell)}_{r,n}\right),
    \label{eq:method-imm-fused-state}\\
    \mathbf{P}_{r,n}
    &=
    \sum_{\ell\in\mathcal{Q}}
    \mu^{(\ell)}_{r,n}
    \left[
    \mathbf{J}_{\ell\rightarrow\mathrm{c}}
    \mathbf{P}^{(\ell)}_{r,n}
    \mathbf{J}_{\ell\rightarrow\mathrm{c}}^{\mathsf T}
    +\boldsymbol{\delta}^{(\ell)}_{r,n}
    \left(\boldsymbol{\delta}^{(\ell)}_{r,n}\right)^{\mathsf T}
    \right],
    \label{eq:method-imm-fused-covariance}
\end{align}
where \(\boldsymbol{\delta}^{(\ell)}_{r,n}\) is the displacement between the
transformed mode-conditioned estimate and the fused state.  IMM thus adjusts the
relative weight of straight and turning motion from the current innovation,
rather than permanently assigning a vehicle to either model.

Before an update, predicted tracks and current detections are screened by the
squared Mahalanobis innovation distance
\begin{equation}
    D^2_{r,i,n}
    =
    \left(\mathbf{z}_{i,n}-\widehat{\mathbf{z}}_{r,n|n-1}\right)^{\mathsf T}
    \mathbf{S}^{-1}_{r,n}
    \left(\mathbf{z}_{i,n}-\widehat{\mathbf{z}}_{r,n|n-1}\right)
    \leq \gamma_{\mathrm{trk}}.
    \label{eq:method-track-gate}
\end{equation}
The surviving pairs are resolved by one-to-one Hungarian assignment using their
innovation costs.  A matched detection updates the kinematic state, mode
probabilities, track confidence, observation history, and current beam-class
posterior \(\mathbf{p}^{\mathrm{obs}}_{r,n}\).  An unmatched track can be
retained temporarily, but its predicted location alone never produces a formal
beam candidate.  Tracking consequently maintains temporal continuity without
substituting for a communication measurement or target-to-user association.

\section{Measurement-Assisted Target-to-User Association}
\label{sec:method-measurement-association}

The beam-class posterior learned in
Section~\ref{sec:method-perception-learning} is indexed by a currently observed
physical track, whereas beam management acts on a VUE.  Conventional beam
measurements are therefore used to establish the target-to-user association
between these two domains.  The method first determines a partial UE--track
mapping in a multi-candidate scene and then uses the association-acceptance state
and current-track observation to determine whether the CSI-RS measurement set
may be reduced.

\subsection{Initial Association from Beam Measurements}
\label{subsec:method-beam-measurement-association}

An association event occurs at initial access, after a serving-cell change, or
when an existing UE--track association is no longer adequately supported.  The
conventional SSB and CSI-RS procedure provides the serving SSB \(s_{b,u,n}\) and
a VUE-indexed CSI-RS beam measurement \(\kappa_{b,u,n}\) for every affected VUE
\(u\).  In contrast, a currently observed track \(r\) provides the beam-class
posterior \(\mathbf{p}^{\mathrm{obs}}_{r,n}\) from the joint perception network.
Its entry \(p^{\mathrm{obs}}_{r,n}(k)\) is the classifier score assigned to beam
\(k\) for the physical target represented by track \(r\).

The compatibility between a VUE measurement and a track is quantified by the
normalised negative log-posterior cost
\begin{equation}
    c^{\mathrm{assoc}}_{u,r,n}
    =
    -\frac{\log\!\left(p^{\mathrm{obs}}_{r,n}\bigl(\kappa_{b,u,n}\bigr)+\epsilon\right)}{\log M_{\mathrm{CSI}}},
    \label{eq:method-association-cost}
\end{equation}
where \(\epsilon>0\) is a numerical constant.  A smaller value indicates that
the beam selected by conventional measurement receives a higher posterior
probability from the track-level classifier.  The beam-prediction output is thus
converted into a communication-grounded association score without requiring the
perception network to classify a VUE identity.

The parent relation between CSI-RS and SSB beams, together with the
current-observation requirement, defines the feasible edge set
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{assoc}}_{b,n}
    =
    \left\{(u,r)\ \middle|\ \varphi_b\bigl(\kappa_{b,u,n}\bigr)=s_{b,u,n},\
    \ r\ \text{is currently observed}\right\}.
    \label{eq:method-association-feasibility}
\end{equation}
Let \(\sigma\) assign each affected VUE either to one feasible track or to a
dummy unmatched column with cost \(c_{\emptyset}\).  The one-to-one association
is obtained from
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{\sigma}
    =
    \underset{\sigma}{\arg\min}
    \sum_{u\in\mathcal{U}^{\mathrm{evt}}_b[n]}c^{\mathrm{assoc}}_{u,\sigma(u),n},
    \qquad
    \sigma(u)\in\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{obs}}_{b,n}\cup\{\emptyset\},
    \label{eq:method-association-assignment}
\end{equation}
subject to the feasible edges and the one-to-one constraint on real tracks.  It
is solved by the Hungarian algorithm after augmentation with dummy columns.
For a real assigned pair \((u,r)\), define the alternative set for VUE \(u\) as
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{alt}}_{u,r,n}
    & =
    \left\{r'\in\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{obs}}_{b,n}\setminus\{r\}
    \ \middle|\ (u,r')\in\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{assoc}}_{b,n}\right\}
    \cup\{\emptyset\},
    \label{eq:method-association-alternatives}\\
    \Delta_{u,r,n}
    & =
    \min_{r'\in\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{alt}}_{u,r,n}}
    c^{\mathrm{assoc}}_{u,r',n}
    -c^{\mathrm{assoc}}_{u,r,n},
    \qquad
    c^{\mathrm{assoc}}_{u,\emptyset,n}=c_{\emptyset}.
    \label{eq:method-association-margin}
\end{align}
The quantity \(\Delta_{u,r,n}\) is the cost separation between the assigned
track and the best feasible alternative for the same VUE.  A larger value
indicates a less ambiguous local association.  The association-acceptance state
is then defined as
\begin{equation}
    A_{u,r,n}
    =
    \mathbf{1}\!\left\{c^{\mathrm{assoc}}_{u,r,n}\leq\tau_{\mathrm{assoc}},\
    \Delta_{u,r,n}\geq\tau_{\mathrm{margin}}\right\}.
    \label{eq:method-association-acceptance}
\end{equation}
Accordingly, \(\pi_{b,n}(u)=r\) when \(A_{u,r,n}=1\), and
\(\pi_{b,n}(u)=\varnothing\) otherwise.  The unmatched option is essential.  It
prevents an ambiguous multi-candidate scene from forcing a sensing target onto
the wrong communication user.

The association procedure excludes ground-truth identities and never maps a
predicted BEV position directly to a codebook beam.  Direction-only and
Top-\(K\)-membership rules are retained as association ablations; the proposed
rule instead uses agreement between a predicted beam-class posterior and a
VUE-indexed conventional measurement.  Once accepted, the UE--track association
is maintained through the corresponding perception track and is reassessed when
the supporting sensing or communication evidence changes.

\subsection{Association-Gated Candidate Selection}
\label{subsec:method-association-gated-selection}

An accepted UE--track association permits candidate reduction only while the
corresponding track is present in the current observation.  Let
\(G_{u,n}\in\{0,1\}\) denote the candidate-use indicator for VUE \(u\).  For
the track selected by the partial mapping \(\pi_{b,n}\), it is defined directly
from the association-acceptance state as
\begin{equation}
    G_{u,n}
    =
    \begin{cases}
        A_{u,r,n}, & \pi_{b,n}(u)=r\in
        \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{obs}}_{b,n},\\
        0, & \text{otherwise}.
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:method-association-gate}
\end{equation}
Here, \(A_{u,r,n}\) is the acceptance decision in
Equation~\eqref{eq:method-association-acceptance}.  Between association events,
the accepted mapping is propagated with the corresponding perception track and
is invalidated when that mapping no longer holds.  Association acceptance thus
determines whether the mapping is admissible, while current observation ensures
that candidate selection uses a fresh target-level beam posterior.

When \(G_{u,n}=1\), the current observation supplies a sensing-assisted
candidate set.  Otherwise, conventional refinement remains active.
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{C}_{b,u,n}
    =
    \begin{cases}
        \operatorname{TopK}_{K_{\mathrm{scan}}}\left(\mathbf{p}^{\mathrm{obs}}_{\pi_{b,n}(u),n}\right),& G_{u,n}=1,\\
        \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{conv}}_{b,u,n},& G_{u,n}=0.
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:method-candidate-policy}
\end{equation}
Thus, a predicted-only track can preserve state-estimation continuity but cannot alone initiate a reduced CSI-RS scan.

The measurement width can adapt to the concentration of the current posterior.  Let \(s_1\geq\cdots\geq s_{K_{\max}}\) be its leading scores.  For minimum width \(K_{\min}\) and score-mass target \(\zeta\), the selected width is
\begin{equation}
    K^{\star}_{\mathrm{scan}}
    =
    \min\left\{k\ \middle|\ k\geq K_{\min},\
    \frac{\sum_{j=1}^{k}s_j}{\sum_{j=1}^{K_{\max}}s_j}\geq\zeta\right\}.
    \label{eq:method-adaptive-scan}
\end{equation}
Diffuse posteriors consequently retain more CSI-RS candidates, whereas
concentrated posteriors permit a narrower scan.  In both cases, the posterior
determines only which beams are measured.  The service beam is selected from the
measured candidate set according to Equation~\eqref{eq:system-service-beam}.  If
the association-gated condition is not satisfied, the framework reverts to
conventional refinement and thereby retains a recovery path.

This association-to-candidate interface prevents an unaccepted mapping or a
prediction-only track from directly constraining a VUE's measurement set.  Its
system consequences are evaluated jointly through association coverage,
sensing-information use, fallback frequency, candidate recall, CSI-RS overhead,
and post-RZF rate.

\section{Algorithm Summary and Complexity}
\label{sec:method-algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:method-framework} summarises the procedure at a target BS.
It distinguishes association events from ordinary maintenance epochs.
Association events use conventional measurements to establish or recover a
VUE--track mapping; maintenance epochs use that mapping for candidate reduction
only while it remains accepted and its track is currently observed.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Measurement-assisted sensing beam management at target BS \(b\)}
\label{alg:method-framework}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Multimodal observation \(\mathcal{O}_{b,n}\), active VUE set
\(\mathcal{U}_b[n]\), previous track set \(\mathcal{R}_{b,n-1}\), previous
association map \(\pi_{b,n-1}\), and communication measurements when an
association event occurs
\ENSURE Updated tracks \(\mathcal{R}_{b,n}\), association map \(\pi_{b,n}\), and
CSI-RS candidate sets \(\{\mathcal{C}_{b,u,n}\}_{u\in\mathcal{U}_b[n]}\)
\STATE Construct the distributed multimodal BEV feature \(\mathbf{F}_{b,n}\).
\STATE Decode target-indexed detections and beam-class posteriors
\(\{(d_{b,i,n},\mathbf{p}_{b,i,n})\}_i\).
    \STATE Mix CV and CT hypotheses, then predict existing tracks using the IMM.
    \STATE Gate and assign current detections to tracks, then update the matched
    states, mode probabilities, confidence, observation history, and current
    beam-class posteriors.
\IF{an initial-access, serving-cell-change, or reassociation event occurs}
    \STATE Obtain conventional SSB/CSI-RS measurements for the affected VUEs.
    \STATE Form feasible UE--track pairs using SSB-parent consistency.
    \STATE Solve the beam-posterior assignment with unmatched alternatives.
    \STATE Apply association acceptance tests and update \(\pi_{b,n}\).
\ENDIF
\FOR{each \(u\in\mathcal{U}_b[n]\)}
    \IF{\(G_{u,n}=1\)}
        \STATE Derive fixed or adaptive sensing candidates from the current
        target posterior.
    \ELSE
        \STATE Use conventional refinement candidates.
    \ENDIF
    \STATE Measure the selected CSI-RS candidates and choose the service beam
    from the measured quality.
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The principal computational terms have distinct scaling behaviour.  BEV feature
construction depends on the number of camera views, point-cloud observations,
and BEV resolution.  Joint inference produces one \(M_{\mathrm{CSI}}\)-way
beam-class posterior per decoded target; its target-level output therefore scales with
the number of detections and the 192-beam codebook.  Detection--track and
UE--track assignments operate on their respective gated candidate sets, and a
standard Hungarian solver has cubic complexity in the larger side of each
assignment.  IMM propagation is linear in the number of active tracks and
motion modes.  In contrast, \(K_{\mathrm{scan}}\) controls air-interface
measurement width rather than sensing-network computation.

This decomposition identifies the variables governing the framework's
computational and air-interface scaling.  Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments} examines
the resulting perception, association, candidate-selection, and communication
performance under the system assumptions defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:system-model}.
