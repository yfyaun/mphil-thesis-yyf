\chapter{Proposed Sensing-Assisted Beam Management Framework}
\label{ch:methodology}

This chapter specifies the proposed sensing-assisted beam-management framework
under the system model of Chapter~\ref{ch:system-model}.  The framework is
organised around a strict separation between a physical vehicle observed by the
sensing system and a VUE addressed by the communication system.  Multimodal
sensing first produces target-level evidence in a target-BS-centred BEV
representation.  Conventional communication measurements then establish the
target-to-user association required to use that evidence, and CSI-RS candidate
reduction is permitted only while this association is both reliable and
supported by a current observation.  Sensing therefore ranks measurements; it
does not replace CSI-RS-based service-beam selection.

The proposed method comprises four coupled functions: distributed multimodal
BEV representation, joint vehicle detection and beam-quality learning,
perception-track maintenance with measurement-assisted target-to-user
association, and reliability-gated candidate selection.  The sensing functions
operate on unbound physical targets, whereas the association and candidate
selection functions connect their outputs to the NR-oriented beam-management
procedure.  The resource model and system-level evaluation of this procedure
are specified in Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments}.

\section{Framework Architecture}
\label{sec:method-framework}

For target BS \(b\) at sensing epoch \(n\), let \(\mathcal{O}_{b,n}\) denote
the multimodal observation defined in Section~\ref{sec:system-sensing}.  The
sensing network maps this observation to a set of vehicle detections and their
associated beam-quality distributions:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{G}_{\theta}(\mathcal{O}_{b,n})
    \longmapsto
    \mathcal{D}_{b,n}
    =
    \left\{
    \bigl(d_{b,i,n},\mathbf{p}_{b,i,n}\bigr)
    \right\}_{i=1}^{N^{\mathrm{det}}_{b,n}},
    \label{eq:method-sensing-output}
\end{equation}
where \(d_{b,i,n}\) is an unbound vehicle observation and
\(\mathbf{p}_{b,i,n}\in[0,1]^{M_{\mathrm{CSI}}}\) is a normalised distribution
over the CSI-RS codebook.  Neither quantity contains a VUE identifier.  A
per-BS multi-object tracker converts the detection sequence into a track set
\(\mathcal{R}_{b,n}\).  At an association event, conventional communication
measurements provide the VUE-indexed evidence required to associate active VUEs
with currently observed tracks.  The resulting partial mapping is
\begin{equation}
    \pi_{b,n}:\mathcal{U}_b[n]\longrightarrow
    \mathcal{R}_{b,n}\cup\{\varnothing\},
    \label{eq:method-binding-map}
\end{equation}
where the null value denotes that no sensing-derived hint is accepted for the
VUE.

The end-to-end decision path can consequently be summarised as
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{O}_{b,n}
    \longrightarrow
    \bigl\{d_{b,i,n},\mathbf{p}_{b,i,n}\bigr\}_{i}
    \longrightarrow
    \mathcal{R}_{b,n}
    \longrightarrow
    \pi_{b,n}
    \longrightarrow
    \mathcal{C}_{b,u,n},
    \label{eq:method-decision-path}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{C}_{b,u,n}\) is either a sensing-derived CSI-RS candidate set
or a conventional refinement set.  This ordering follows from the different
indices of the two domains: a beam distribution is target-indexed, whereas
measurement, feedback, and scheduling are VUE-indexed.  The association layer
is consequently required before a target-level ranking can influence a
user-specific measurement decision.

The framework is further defined by three information boundaries.  First, the
perception network uses only calibrated camera and point-cloud observations;
link coordinates, VUE identities, and oracle beam labels are excluded from its
forward input.  Second, the tracker maintains the temporal identity of a
physical target rather than inferring a communication identity.  Third, the
beam-management module uses a prediction only to select CSI-RS measurements;
the measured CSI-RS quality selects the service beam.  These boundaries make
the contributions of perception, association, and measurement reduction
separately identifiable in the evaluation.

\section{Distributed Multimodal BEV Representation}
\label{sec:method-bev}

The BEV representation provides a common spatial interface for distributed
visual observations, BS-local ISAC sensing, vehicle detection, and beam-quality
learning.  Let \(\mathcal{J}_b=\{b\}\cup\mathcal{N}_b\) denote the target BS
and its neighbouring SNs.  For each node \(j\in\mathcal{J}_b\), calibrated
multiview images are encoded and lifted into the BEV frame of BS \(b\):
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{cam}}_{j\rightarrow b,n}
    =
    \Phi_{\mathrm{img}}\!
    \left(
    \mathcal{I}_{j,n};\mathbf{T}_{j\rightarrow b}
    \right),
    \label{eq:method-camera-lift}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{T}_{j\rightarrow b}\) denotes the calibration information that
expresses node \(j\)'s visual evidence in the target-BS-centred coordinate
system.  The representation requires that all node features be expressed in
these common BEV coordinates before aggregation, independent of the particular
image encoder or view-transformation implementation.

The distributed visual evidence is aggregated as
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{cam}}_{b,n}
    =
    \Phi_{\mathrm{node}}
    \left(
    \left\{
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{cam}}_{j\rightarrow b,n},
    \mathbf{m}^{\mathrm{cam}}_{j,n}
    \right\}_{j\in\mathcal{J}_b}
    \right),
    \label{eq:method-node-aggregation}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{m}^{\mathrm{cam}}_{j,n}\) captures view availability or
reliability.  The study compares node aggregation mechanisms, including uniform
aggregation and learned cross-node attention, within the common operator
\(\Phi_{\mathrm{node}}\).  Across these mechanisms, neighbouring nodes serve the
same algorithmic purpose: they supplement the target-BS view when its coverage
is incomplete or affected by occlusion.

The BS-local ISAC point cloud is independently encoded on the same grid,
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{isac}}_{b,n}
    =
    \Phi_{\mathrm{pc}}\!
    \left(\mathcal{P}_{b,n}\right).
    \label{eq:method-isac-encoding}
\end{equation}
The camera and point-cloud representations are combined by a modality-fusion
operator,
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}_{b,n}
    =
    \Phi_{\mathrm{modal}}
    \left(
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{cam}}_{b,n},
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{isac}}_{b,n};
    \mathbf{m}^{\mathrm{modal}}_{b,n}
    \right),
    \label{eq:method-modal-fusion}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{m}^{\mathrm{modal}}_{b,n}\) represents modality availability or
confidence.  Modality gating can downweight unreliable evidence, while node
aggregation preserves complementary visual observations.  Neither operation is
used to infer a communication identity; their role is to construct a
target-level scene representation with improved observability.

The shared BEV representation aligns a detected vehicle and its beam-quality
supervision with the local service region of BS \(b\).  It also enables
cross-view evidence to influence both detection and beam-quality prediction
before target-level outputs are decoded.  The resulting feature map
\(\mathbf{F}_{b,n}\) is therefore shared by the two prediction tasks described
next.

\section{Joint Detection and Beam-Quality Learning}
\label{sec:method-joint-learning}

\subsection{Target-Level Prediction}
\label{subsec:method-target-prediction}

The joint prediction head maps \(\mathbf{F}_{b,n}\) to a vehicle-centre
heatmap, target attributes, and a spatially dense CSI beam-quality map:
\begin{equation}
    \left(
    \mathbf{H}_{b,n},
    \mathbf{A}_{b,n},
    \mathbf{P}_{b,n}
    \right)
    =
    \Phi_{\mathrm{joint}}\!
    \left(\mathbf{F}_{b,n}\right),
    \label{eq:method-joint-head}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{H}_{b,n}\) is the centre heatmap, \(\mathbf{A}_{b,n}\)
contains geometric or motion-related attributes, and
\(\mathbf{P}_{b,n}\) contains \(M_{\mathrm{CSI}}=192\) beam scores at every BEV
location.  Local maxima of \(\mathbf{H}_{b,n}\) define the vehicle centres.  At
the \(i\)-th decoded centre, the target-level output is
\begin{equation}
    d_{b,i,n}
    =
    \left(
    \widehat{\mathbf{r}}_{b,i,n},
    \widehat{\mathbf{a}}_{b,i,n},
    \widehat{c}_{b,i,n}
    \right),\qquad
    \mathbf{p}_{b,i,n}
    =
    \operatorname{Sample}
    \left(
    \mathbf{P}_{b,n},
    \widehat{\mathbf{r}}_{b,i,n}
    \right).
    \label{eq:method-target-output}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\widehat{\mathbf{r}}_{b,i,n}\) is the estimated BEV centre,
\(\widehat{\mathbf{a}}_{b,i,n}\) contains available size, heading, and velocity
attributes, and \(\widehat{c}_{b,i,n}\) is the detection confidence.  Sampling
\(\mathbf{P}_{b,n}\) at the decoded centre converts the dense map into a
distribution specific to the corresponding physical target.  Consequently,
several simultaneously visible vehicles can receive distinct candidate rankings
from a single shared BEV representation.

During training, the BS--UE link coordinate identifies the BEV location at
which beam supervision is sampled.  It acts solely as a supervision index: it
is excluded from the forward model and is not inverted to obtain a beam index at
inference time.  The prediction head also receives neither the VUE identity nor
an oracle track identity.  Equation~\eqref{eq:method-target-output} therefore
defines an unbound target-level output that remains suitable for the subsequent
association stage.

\subsection{Distributional Beam Supervision}
\label{subsec:method-distributional-supervision}

For a supervised BS--UE link, let \(g_{b,u,n}(k)\) be the CSI beam quality in
Equation~\eqref{eq:system-beam-quality}.  The physical power vector is
converted to a soft target distribution,
\begin{equation}
    q_{b,u,n}(k)
    =
    \operatorname{softmax}\!\left(
    \frac{
    10\log_{10}g_{b,u,n}(k)
    -
    \max_j 10\log_{10}g_{b,u,n}(j)}
    {T}
    \right),
    \qquad
    k=1,\ldots,M_{\mathrm{CSI}},
    \label{eq:method-soft-beam-target}
\end{equation}
where \(T\) controls the concentration of the target distribution.  In contrast
to a strongest-beam label, \(q_{b,u,n}\) preserves the relative usefulness of
multiple beams.  This information is required by CSI-RS candidate selection,
for which a predicted list can be useful even when its leading entry is not the
unique strongest physical beam.

Let \(\mathbf{z}_{b,i,n}\) be the unnormalised prediction logits at a supervised
target location, and let
\(\mathbf{p}_{b,i,n}=\operatorname{softmax}(\mathbf{z}_{b,i,n})\).  The beam
loss is
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{beam}}
    =
    D_{\mathrm{KL}}
    \left(
    \mathbf{q}_{b,u,n}\Vert\mathbf{p}_{b,i,n}
    \right)
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{rank}}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{hard-rank}}.
    \label{eq:method-beam-loss}
\end{equation}
The first term matches the predicted and physical quality distributions.  The
second penalises ranking violations between the strongest physical beam and a
set \(\mathcal{H}^{-}_{b,u,n}\) of substantially weaker hard-negative beams:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{hard-rank}}
    =
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal{H}^{-}_{b,u,n}|}
    \sum_{k\in\mathcal{H}^{-}_{b,u,n}}
    \ell_{\mathrm{rank}}
    \left(
    z_{b,i,n}(k^\star_{b,u,n}),
    z_{b,i,n}(k)
    \right),
    \label{eq:method-hard-rank-loss}
\end{equation}
where \(k^\star_{b,u,n}\) is the strongest physical CSI beam and
\(\ell_{\mathrm{rank}}(\cdot,\cdot)\) is a pairwise ranking penalty.  The
ranking term suppresses score inversions in which a substantially weaker beam
outranks the strongest beam in the exported candidate list.

The complete training objective combines the detection and beam terms,
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{joint}}
    =
    \lambda_{\mathrm{hm}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{hm}}
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{attr}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{attr}}
    +
    \lambda_{\mathrm{beam}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{beam}},
    \label{eq:method-joint-loss}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{hm}}\) and
\(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{attr}}\) supervise the vehicle-centre and target-attribute
outputs, respectively.  The objective learns a calibrated, candidate-oriented
quality distribution rather than an unmeasured service-beam decision.  Its
effect is assessed through detection-conditioned beam ranking, power retention,
and calibration metrics.

Three list lengths serve distinct functions.  The model exports the full
\(K_{\mathrm{model}}=M_{\mathrm{CSI}}\)-entry distribution.  Association may
inspect a ranked subset of length \(K_{\mathrm{bind}}\), whereas CSI-RS measures
only \(K_{\mathrm{scan}}\) beams after a binding is accepted.  Preserving this
distinction prevents an information-rich prediction from being interpreted as
an equally large measurement budget.

\section{Target-to-User Association and Track Maintenance}
\label{sec:method-association-tracking}

The sensing network observes physical targets, whereas the communication system
measures and schedules VUEs.  The two domains are connected without requiring
the perception model to recognise a VUE identity.  Track maintenance first
provides temporally coherent target-level evidence; measurement-assisted
association then anchors that evidence to a VUE when the conventional
beam-management procedure provides the required communication observation.

\subsection{Perception Track Maintenance}
\label{subsec:method-track-maintenance}

Tracks are maintained independently for each split, weather condition, and
target BS.  Let \(r\in\mathcal{R}_{b,n}\) denote a perception track with
kinematic state
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}_{r,n}
    =
    [x_{r,n},y_{r,n},v_{x,r,n},v_{y,r,n}]^{\mathsf{T}}.
    \label{eq:method-track-state}
\end{equation}
The state describes the BEV motion of a physical vehicle.  CV Kalman filtering,
constant-turn extended Kalman filtering, and an interacting multiple model
(IMM) combining CV and constant-turn modes provide the tracking comparators.
For the IMM, the mode-conditioned predictions are mixed as
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{\mathbf{x}}_{r,n|n-1}
    =
    \sum_{q\in\{\mathrm{CV},\mathrm{CT}\}}
    \mu^{(q)}_{r,n|n-1}
    \widehat{\mathbf{x}}^{(q)}_{r,n|n-1},
    \qquad
    \sum_q\mu^{(q)}_{r,n|n-1}=1,
    \label{eq:method-imm-state}
\end{equation}
which accommodates both approximately straight and turning vehicle motion.  The
tracker remains independent of VUE identity: it propagates the state of a
physical target between sensing observations.

At epoch \(n\), predicted tracks and current detections are paired using a
gated one-to-one assignment.  A generic cost is
\begin{equation}
    c^{\mathrm{trk}}_{r,i,n}
    =
    \Psi_{\mathrm{trk}}
    \left(
    \widehat{\mathbf{x}}_{r,n|n-1},
    d_{b,i,n},
    \mathbf{p}_{b,i,n}
    \right),
    \label{eq:method-track-cost}
\end{equation}
where \(\Psi_{\mathrm{trk}}\) is based primarily on spatial innovation and
motion consistency and can incorporate detection confidence and
beam-distribution consistency.  Pairs outside the motion gate are discarded
before the Hungarian assignment.  A matched detection updates the state,
confidence, observation count, beam distribution, and hint age of the
corresponding track.  An unmatched track may remain in the state estimator, but
its predicted location never generates a formal beam candidate; only a current
observation can provide the beam evidence used by the candidate policy.

\subsection{Measurement-Assisted Association}
\label{subsec:method-measurement-association}

An association event is triggered at initial access, after a serving-cell
change, or following a loss of binding reliability.  Conventional SSB and
CSI-RS measurements then supply VUE-indexed evidence.  Let \(s_{b,u,n}\)
denote the serving SSB of VUE \(u\), and let \(\kappa_{b,u,n}\) denote a CSI-RS
beam selected from the conventional measurement.  For a currently observed
track \(r\) carrying beam distribution \(\mathbf{p}_{r,n}\), the association
cost is
\begin{equation}
    c^{\mathrm{bind}}_{u,r,n}
    =
    -\frac{
    \log p_{r,n}\bigl(\kappa_{b,u,n}\bigr)}
    {\log M_{\mathrm{CSI}}}.
    \label{eq:method-binding-cost}
\end{equation}
The cost treats the target-level distribution as a beam likelihood: a lower
value indicates stronger agreement between the communication measurement and
the sensing evidence.

The parent relation between CSI-RS and SSB beams first determines the feasible
edge set:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{bind}}_{b,n}
    =
    \left\{
    (u,r):
    \varphi_b(\kappa_{b,u,n})=s_{b,u,n},\
    r\ \text{is currently observed}
    \right\}.
    \label{eq:method-binding-feasibility}
\end{equation}
The restricted cost matrix is augmented with dummy unmatched columns and solved
by a one-to-one Hungarian assignment.  A proposed match is accepted only if its
likelihood cost, assignment margin, and non-ground-truth reliability evidence
satisfy the association rule.  Otherwise, \(\pi_{b,n}(u)=\varnothing\) and
conventional refinement remains in force.  The unmatched alternative is
essential in a multi-candidate scene because it prevents a forced and
potentially incorrect VUE--track binding.

The association rule excludes ground-truth identities from the cost matrix,
does not invert a predicted BEV location into a codebook beam, and does not
treat a perception track as a recognised VUE.  Direction-only and
Top-\(K\)-membership matching serve as association ablations; the proposed rule
instead combines the predicted beam distribution with communication-side
measurements.  Once accepted, a binding is propagated with the matched
perception track and is reassessed whenever tracking or reliability evidence no
longer supports it.

\section{Reliability-Gated Candidate Selection}
\label{sec:method-candidate-selection}

A valid UE--track association alone is insufficient to justify CSI-RS candidate
reduction.  Let \(G_{u,n}\in\{0,1\}\) be the reliability gate for VUE \(u\).
Its decision uses only information available to the BS: binding confidence,
assignment margin, current detection and track confidence, hint age, SSB-parent
consistency, observation history, and beam-score concentration.  Denoting these
quantities collectively by \(\boldsymbol{\xi}_{u,n}\), the gate is written as
\begin{equation}
    G_{u,n}=\Gamma(\boldsymbol{\xi}_{u,n}).
    \label{eq:method-reliability-gate}
\end{equation}
This formulation separates the calibration of decision thresholds from
held-out system evaluation and excludes ground-truth information from the
online decision.

When the gate accepts a current observation, the associated target distribution
defines the sensing-assisted candidate set:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{C}_{b,u,n}
    =
    \begin{cases}
        \operatorname{TopK}_{K_{\mathrm{scan}}}
        \left(
        \mathbf{p}^{\mathrm{obs}}_{\pi_{b,n}(u),n}
        \right),&
        G_{u,n}=1,\\
        \mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{conv}}_{b,u,n},&
        G_{u,n}=0,
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:method-candidate-policy}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{p}^{\mathrm{obs}}_{\pi_{b,n}(u),n}\) is refreshed by the current
matched detection.  A prediction-only track therefore cannot generate a
CSI-RS candidate set.

The candidate width can also adapt to the concentration of the current
distribution.  Let \(s_1\geq\cdots\geq s_{K_{\max}}\) denote the leading beam
scores.  For a minimum scan width \(K_{\min}\) and score-mass target \(\zeta\),
the selected width is
\begin{equation}
    K^{\star}_{\mathrm{scan}}
    =
    \min\left\{
    k:
    k\geq K_{\min},\
    \frac{\sum_{j=1}^{k}s_j}
    {\sum_{j=1}^{K_{\max}}s_j}
    \geq\zeta
    \right\}.
    \label{eq:method-adaptive-scan}
\end{equation}
This rule assigns more CSI-RS candidates to diffuse distributions and fewer to
concentrated ones.  It controls measurement width rather than selecting the
service beam.  After CSI-RS measurements are collected, the service beam is
selected from the measured candidate set as in
Equation~\eqref{eq:system-service-beam}.  When the gate rejects a hint, the
conventional procedure supplies both the candidate set and the recovery path.

The gate enforces a conservative use of sensing by exposing association errors,
missed detections, and stale predictions to conventional recovery rather than
allowing them to determine a reduced measurement set.  Accordingly, the
evaluation reports hint usage, fallback frequency, association coverage,
candidate recall, CSI-RS overhead, and post-RZF rate jointly.

\section{Algorithm Summary and Complexity}
\label{sec:method-algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:method-framework} summarises the procedure at a target BS.
It distinguishes association events from ordinary maintenance epochs.
Association events use conventional measurements to establish or recover a
VUE--track mapping; maintenance epochs retain that mapping only when current
sensing evidence and the reliability gate support it.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Reliability-gated sensing-assisted beam management at target BS \(b\)}
\label{alg:method-framework}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Multimodal observation \(\mathcal{O}_{b,n}\), active VUE set
\(\mathcal{U}_b[n]\), previous track set \(\mathcal{R}_{b,n-1}\), previous
binding map \(\pi_{b,n-1}\), and communication measurements when an association
event occurs
\ENSURE Updated tracks \(\mathcal{R}_{b,n}\), binding map \(\pi_{b,n}\), and
CSI-RS candidate sets \(\{\mathcal{C}_{b,u,n}\}_{u\in\mathcal{U}_b[n]}\)
\STATE Construct the distributed multimodal BEV feature \(\mathbf{F}_{b,n}\).
\STATE Decode unbound detections and target-level distributions
\(\{(d_{b,i,n},\mathbf{p}_{b,i,n})\}_i\).
\STATE Predict existing perception tracks and associate them with current
detections using gated Hungarian assignment.
\STATE Update matched tracks, their confidence, observation history, hint age,
and current beam distributions.
\IF{an initial-access, serving-cell-change, or reassociation event occurs}
    \STATE Obtain conventional SSB/CSI-RS measurements for the affected VUEs.
    \STATE Form feasible UE--track pairs using SSB-parent consistency.
    \STATE Solve the beam-likelihood assignment with unmatched alternatives.
    \STATE Accept only reliable matches and update \(\pi_{b,n}\).
\ENDIF
\FOR{each \(u\in\mathcal{U}_b[n]\)}
    \IF{\(\pi_{b,n}(u)\) is accepted, currently observed, and \(G_{u,n}=1\)}
        \STATE Derive fixed or adaptive sensing candidates from the current
        target distribution.
    \ELSE
        \STATE Use conventional refinement candidates.
    \ENDIF
    \STATE Measure the selected CSI-RS candidates and choose the service beam
    from the measured quality.
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The principal computational terms have distinct scaling behaviour.  BEV feature
construction depends on the number of camera views, point-cloud observations,
and BEV resolution.  Joint inference produces one \(M_{\mathrm{CSI}}\)-way
distribution per decoded target; its target-level output therefore scales with
the number of detections and the 192-beam codebook.  Detection--track and
UE--track assignments operate on their respective gated candidate sets, and a
standard Hungarian solver has cubic complexity in the larger side of each
assignment.  IMM propagation is linear in the number of active tracks and
motion modes.  In contrast, \(K_{\mathrm{scan}}\) controls air-interface
measurement width rather than sensing-network computation.

This decomposition identifies the variables governing the framework's
algorithmic and measurement burden without asserting an implementation-specific
latency.  Chapter~\ref{ch:experiments} evaluates the resulting perception,
association, candidate-selection, and communication consequences under a common
frozen protocol.
