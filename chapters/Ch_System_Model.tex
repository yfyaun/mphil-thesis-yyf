\chapter{System Model}
\label{ch:system-model}

This chapter establishes the system abstraction for distributed multimodal
sensing-assisted beam management in a vehicular network.  It distinguishes the
physical vehicle observed by the sensing system from the vehicle user equipment
(VUE) addressed by the communication system, and specifies how the two domains
are coupled through communication measurements.  The presentation proceeds from
the local network architecture and sensing observations to the downlink channel,
CSI acquisition, precoding, and resource-aware rate models.  The learning,
tracking, and association mechanisms that operate on these system interfaces are
developed in Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}.

\section{Network Architecture}
\label{sec:system-network}

Consider a roadside vehicular network with a set of base stations (BSs)
\(\mathcal{B}\).  The formulation is centred on a target BS \(b\in\mathcal{B}\),
which serves the VUEs in its local service region and executes the corresponding
beam-management decisions.  A set \(\mathcal{N}_b\) of neighbouring distributed
sensing nodes (SNs) provides additional camera views of the same road region.
The SNs are sensing-only collaborators and do not establish independent service
cells in the model.  Thus, all communication measurements,
beam decisions, and association states discussed below are local to the target
BS.

Let \(\mathcal{U}_b[n]\) denote the active VUE set of BS \(b\) at sensing epoch
\(n\).  A member \(u\in\mathcal{U}_b[n]\) has a communication-side identifier
used for synchronisation, measurement reporting, and downlink transmission.  In
contrast, the sensing system produces unlabelled physical
targets.  A vehicle that is visible to the sensors is therefore not automatically
known to be a particular VUE, and a VUE can be temporarily unobserved because of
occlusion or a sensing miss.  This distinction makes target-to-user association
an explicit part of the system model rather than an implicit consequence of
detection.

The index \(n\) refers to a sensing epoch, at which observations from the BS and
its SNs are aligned to a common local bird's-eye-view (BEV) frame.  The frame is
anchored at the target BS and covers its local service region.  A VUE entering a
new serving cell establishes a new local association there; no global identity
is assumed to persist across BSs.  The relation between sensing epochs and the
radio timing used for beam management is specified in
Section~\ref{subsec:system-beam-management}.

\section{Multimodal and Distributed Sensing Model}
\label{sec:system-sensing}

At sensing epoch \(n\), the observations available to BS \(b\) combine multiview
images from the BS and its neighbouring SNs with an ISAC-derived point cloud
available locally at the BS.  We write
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{O}_{b,n}
    = \bigl\{\mathcal{I}_{b,n},\mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{N}_b,n},
    \mathcal{P}_{b,n}\bigr\},
    \label{eq:system-sensing-observation}
\end{equation}
where the three terms respectively denote the target-BS camera observations,
neighbouring-SN camera observations, and the BS-local point cloud.  Sensor
calibration maps every modality into the BEV frame of BS \(b\).  This common
representation permits complementary viewpoints to be used for vehicle
observation, while retaining a BS-centred interpretation of the beam labels.

\subsection{Camera Observations}
\label{subsec:system-camera}

Let \(\mathcal{V}_j\) be the set of cameras mounted at roadside node \(j\).  The
camera observations of the target BS and its collaborating SNs are respectively
written as
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{I}_{b,n} & = \{I_{b,v,n}: v\in\mathcal{V}_b\}, \\
    \mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{N}_b,n} & =
    \{I_{s,v,n}:s\in\mathcal{N}_b,\ v\in\mathcal{V}_s\}.
    \label{eq:system-camera-observations}
\end{align}
Each image is accompanied by fixed camera intrinsics and an extrinsic transform
to the target-BS coordinate system.  The distributed views are not treated as
separate predictions that are combined only after detection.  Instead, they
provide observations of a shared physical scene before the joint detection and
beam-prediction task.  In particular, an SN view can retain evidence of a
vehicle whose target-BS view is partly occluded, whereas the target-BS view
maintains the direct geometric relation to the serving link.

\subsection{ISAC Sensing Model}
\label{subsec:system-isac}

The target BS performs monostatic radio sensing using known symbols embedded in
an ISAC transmission.  On subcarrier \(p\) and sensing symbol \(l\), the
transmitted vector is represented by
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{sen}}_{b,n}[p,l]
    =\sqrt{P^{\mathrm{sen}}_{b,n}}
    \mathbf{f}^{\mathrm{sen}}_{b,n}[p,l]s^{\mathrm{sen}}_{b,n}[p,l],
    \qquad
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[\left|s^{\mathrm{sen}}_{b,n}[p,l]\right|^2\right]=1,
    \label{eq:system-isac-transmit}
\end{equation}
where \(P^{\mathrm{sen}}_{b,n}\),
\(\mathbf{f}^{\mathrm{sen}}_{b,n}[p,l]\), and
\(s^{\mathrm{sen}}_{b,n}[p,l]\) denote the sensing power, transmit beam, and
known sensing symbol, respectively.  The received echo is
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{y}^{\mathrm{echo}}_{b,n}[p,l]
    =\left(\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{sta}}_{b}[p]
    +\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{dyn}}_{b,n}[p,l]\right)
    \mathbf{x}^{\mathrm{sen}}_{b,n}[p,l]
    +\mathbf{z}^{\mathrm{sen}}_{b,n}[p,l],
    \label{eq:system-isac-echo}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{sta}}_{b}[p]\) collects reflections from
stationary roadside structures and \(\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{dyn}}_{b,n}[p,l]\)
contains propagation paths involving moving objects.  The receiver noise is
modelled as circularly symmetric complex Gaussian.

The static component is expressed as
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{sta}}_{b}[p]
    =\sum_{c\in\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{sta}}_b}
    \beta^{\mathrm{sta}}_{b,c}
    e^{-\mathrm{j}2\pi p\Delta f\tau^{\mathrm{sta}}_{b,c}}
    \mathbf{a}^{\mathrm{r}}_b\!\left(\theta^{\mathrm{r}}_{b,c}\right)
    \left(\mathbf{a}^{\mathrm{t}}_b
    \!\left(\theta^{\mathrm{t}}_{b,c}\right)\right)^{\mathrm{H}},
    \label{eq:system-isac-static-channel}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{sta}}_b\) indexes resolvable static reflectors,
\(\beta^{\mathrm{sta}}_{b,c}\) and \(\tau^{\mathrm{sta}}_{b,c}\) are the
path gain and round-trip delay, and \(\mathbf{a}^{\mathrm{t}}_b(\cdot)\) and
\(\mathbf{a}^{\mathrm{r}}_b(\cdot)\) are the transmit and receive array
responses.  Its parameters vary slowly relative to the vehicular targets and
can therefore be estimated from background observations or slow-time averaging.

Let \(\mathcal{Q}^{\mathrm{dyn}}_{b,n}\) denote the moving objects within the
sensing region.  Their target-related channel is decomposed as
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{dyn}}_{b,n}[p,l]
    =\sum_{q\in\mathcal{Q}^{\mathrm{dyn}}_{b,n}}
    \left(\mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{dir}}_{b,q,n}[p,l]
    +\sum_{r\in\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{sec}}_{b,q,n}}
    \mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{sec}}_{b,q,r,n}[p,l]\right).
    \label{eq:system-isac-dynamic-channel}
\end{equation}
The first term is the direct monostatic reflection from object \(q\), whereas
the second comprises target-involving paths with an additional specular or
diffuse environmental reflection.  Both terms follow the same parametric form,
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{dir}}_{b,q,n}[p,l]
    &=\beta^{\mathrm{dir}}_{b,q,n}
    e^{-\mathrm{j}2\pi p\Delta f\tau^{\mathrm{dir}}_{b,q,n}}
    e^{\mathrm{j}2\pi\nu^{\mathrm{dir}}_{b,q,n}lT_{\mathrm{sym}}}
    \mathbf{a}^{\mathrm{r}}_b\!\left(\theta^{\mathrm{r,dir}}_{b,q,n}\right)
    \left(\mathbf{a}^{\mathrm{t}}_b
    \!\left(\theta^{\mathrm{t,dir}}_{b,q,n}\right)\right)^{\mathrm{H}},
    \label{eq:system-isac-direct-path}\\
    \mathbf{H}^{\mathrm{sec}}_{b,q,r,n}[p,l]
    &=\beta^{\mathrm{sec}}_{b,q,r,n}
    e^{-\mathrm{j}2\pi p\Delta f\tau^{\mathrm{sec}}_{b,q,r,n}}
    e^{\mathrm{j}2\pi\nu^{\mathrm{sec}}_{b,q,r,n}lT_{\mathrm{sym}}}
    \mathbf{a}^{\mathrm{r}}_b\!\left(\theta^{\mathrm{r,sec}}_{b,q,r,n}\right)
    \left(\mathbf{a}^{\mathrm{t}}_b
    \!\left(\theta^{\mathrm{t,sec}}_{b,q,r,n}\right)\right)^{\mathrm{H}}.
    \label{eq:system-isac-secondary-path}
\end{align}
The corresponding Doppler terms distinguish these target-related components
from the predominantly stationary background, while the secondary-path index
\(r\) accounts for the fact that a moving vehicle can generate more than one
spatially resolvable return.

Because the transmitted symbols are known, the echo channel can be estimated by
matched filtering or least-squares processing.  After estimating the static
background, conventional clutter suppression yields
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{\mathbf{H}}_{b,n}
    =\widehat{\mathbf{H}}^{\mathrm{echo}}_{b,n}
    -\widehat{\mathbf{H}}^{\mathrm{sta}}_{b},
    \qquad
    Z_{b,n}(\rho,\theta,\nu)
    =\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{RAD}}
    \!\left(\widetilde{\mathbf{H}}_{b,n}\right),
    \label{eq:system-isac-processing}
\end{equation}
where \(\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{RAD}}\) denotes range--angle--Doppler processing.
A cell-averaging constant-false-alarm-rate (CFAR) detector retains a resolution
cell only when
\begin{equation}
    \left|Z_{b,n}(\rho,\theta,\nu)\right|^2
    >\eta^{\mathrm{CFAR}}_{b,n}(\rho,\theta,\nu),
    \label{eq:system-isac-cfar}
\end{equation}
where the threshold is determined from neighbouring cells for a prescribed
false-alarm rate.  The range and angular estimates of the retained cells are
then transformed to the BS-local frame, giving the point cloud
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{P}_{b,n}=
    \{(\mathbf{q}_{b,m,n},a_{b,m,n})\}_{m=1}^{N^{\mathrm{pc}}_{b,n}}.
    \label{eq:system-point-cloud}
\end{equation}
Here, \(\mathbf{q}_{b,m,n}\) is a point position and \(a_{b,m,n}\) may contain
the associated return strength, Doppler, or detector confidence.  Following
clutter suppression and CFAR detection, \(\mathcal{P}_{b,n}\) constitutes the
BS-local radio-sensing observation supplied to the multimodal perception model.

\section{Communication Model}
\label{sec:system-communication}

The communication model specifies the multi-user downlink, beam-domain CSI
acquisition, hybrid precoding, and the radio resources consumed by beam
management.  It thereby relates the selected CSI-RS candidate set to both the
resulting downlink SINR and the fraction of resources available for data
transmission.

\subsection{Downlink Signal and Channel Model}
\label{subsec:system-downlink-model}

Let \(N_b\) denote the number of transmit-antenna elements at BS \(b\).  During
beam-management cycle \(n\), the BS provides multi-user downlink transmission
to the VUE set \(\mathcal{U}_b[n]\).  The downlink channel from BS \(b\) to VUE
\(u\) is denoted by
\(\mathbf{h}_{b,u,n}\in\mathbb{C}^{N_b}\).  Its temporal evolution is
represented by the geometric multipath channel
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{h}_{b,u,n}
    =
    \sum_{\ell=1}^{L_{b,u,n}}
    \alpha_{b,u,\ell,n}
    e^{\mathrm{j}2\pi\nu_{b,u,\ell,n}t_n}
    \mathbf{a}_b(\vartheta_{b,u,\ell,n}),
    \label{eq:system-geometric-channel}
\end{equation}
where \(L_{b,u,n}\) is the number of resolved paths,
\(\alpha_{b,u,\ell,n}\), \(\nu_{b,u,\ell,n}\), and
\(\vartheta_{b,u,\ell,n}\) are the complex gain, Doppler frequency, and
departure angle of path \(\ell\), respectively; \(t_n\) is the reference time
of cycle \(n\); and \(\mathbf{a}_b(\cdot)\) is the transmit-array response.

The BS transmits
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}_{b,n}
    =\sum_{u\in\mathcal{U}_b[n]}
    \sqrt{P_{b,u,n}}\mathbf{w}_{b,u,n}s_{b,u,n},
    \qquad \|\mathbf{w}_{b,u,n}\|_2=1,
    \label{eq:system-downlink-transmit}
\end{equation}
where \(s_{b,u,n}\) is a unit-power data symbol, \(P_{b,u,n}\) is the
transmit power, and \(\mathbf{w}_{b,u,n}\in\mathbb{C}^{N_b}\) is the
unit-norm precoding vector.  The received signal at VUE \(u\) is
\begin{equation}
    y_{b,u,n}
    =
    \sqrt{P_{b,u,n}}\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{H}}_{b,u,n}
    \mathbf{w}_{b,u,n}s_{b,u,n}
    +
    \sum_{v\in\mathcal{U}_b[n]\setminus\{u\}}
    \sqrt{P_{b,v,n}}\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{H}}_{b,u,n}
    \mathbf{w}_{b,v,n}s_{b,v,n}
    +z_{b,u,n},
    \label{eq:system-downlink-signal}
\end{equation}
where \(z_{b,u,n}\sim\mathcal{CN}(0,\sigma^2_{b,u,n})\).  The corresponding
instantaneous downlink SINR is
\begin{equation}
    \gamma_{b,u,n}
    =
    \frac{P_{b,u,n}
    \left|\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{H}}_{b,u,n}\mathbf{w}_{b,u,n}\right|^2}
    {\displaystyle
    \sum_{v\in\mathcal{U}_b[n]\setminus\{u\}}
    P_{b,v,n}
    \left|\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{H}}_{b,u,n}\mathbf{w}_{b,v,n}\right|^2
    +\sigma^2_{b,u,n}}.
    \label{eq:system-downlink-sinr}
\end{equation}
Before accounting for beam-management resources, the corresponding downlink
achievable rate is
\begin{equation}
    R^{\mathrm{DL}}_{b,u,n}
    =B_b\eta_b^{\mathrm{DL}}
    \log_2\!\left(1+\gamma_{b,u,n}\right),
    \label{eq:system-downlink-rate}
\end{equation}
where \(B_b\) is the available bandwidth and \(\eta_b^{\mathrm{DL}}\) is the
downlink resource fraction.  This is the gross rate supported by the selected
precoder; the measurement resources required to obtain that precoder are
incorporated below.

\subsection{CSI Acquisition and Feedback}
\label{subsec:system-csi-feedback}

Accurate CSI is required to construct the downlink precoder.  In an NR-oriented
procedure, the BS transmits known CSI-RS symbols through a beam codebook
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{F}^{\mathrm{CSI}}_b
    =\left\{\mathbf{f}^{\mathrm{CSI}}_{b,k}:
    k=1,\ldots,M_{\mathrm{CSI}}\right\},
    \qquad \left\|\mathbf{f}^{\mathrm{CSI}}_{b,k}\right\|_2=1.
    \label{eq:system-csi-codebook}
\end{equation}
If beam \(k\) carries the unit-power reference symbol \(c_{b,k,n}[l]\), VUE
\(u\) observes
\begin{equation}
    y^{\mathrm{CSI}}_{b,u,k,n}[l]
    =\sqrt{P^{\mathrm{CSI}}_{b,k,n}}
    \underbrace{\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{H}}_{b,u,n}
    \mathbf{f}^{\mathrm{CSI}}_{b,k}}_{h^{\mathrm{eq}}_{b,u,k,n}}
    c_{b,k,n}[l]+z^{\mathrm{CSI}}_{b,u,k,n}[l],
    \label{eq:system-csirs-signal}
\end{equation}
where \(h^{\mathrm{eq}}_{b,u,k,n}\) is the beam-domain equivalent channel.
Correlation with the known reference symbol provides
\(\widehat{h}^{\mathrm{eq}}_{b,u,k,n}\).  A corresponding CSI-RS quality
measure can be written as
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{\gamma}^{\mathrm{CSI}}_{b,u,k,n}
    =\frac{P^{\mathrm{CSI}}_{b,k,n}
    \left|\widehat{h}^{\mathrm{eq}}_{b,u,k,n}\right|^2}
    {\widehat{I}_{b,u,k,n}+\sigma^2_{b,u,n}},
    \label{eq:system-csirs-sinr}
\end{equation}
where \(\widehat{I}_{b,u,k,n}\) represents interference measured on the
reference resource.  Equivalently, the underlying physical beam gain is
\begin{equation}
    g_{b,u,n}(k)
    =
    \left|\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{H}}_{b,u,n}
    \mathbf{f}^{\mathrm{CSI}}_{b,k}\right|^2,
    \qquad k\in\{1,\ldots,M_{\mathrm{CSI}}\},
    \label{eq:system-beam-gain}
\end{equation}
and the strongest physical beam \(\arg\max_k g_{b,u,n}(k)\) supplies the
Top-1 class used to supervise beam prediction, but remains unavailable to the
online decision procedure.  For a measured candidate set
\(\mathcal{C}_{b,u,n}\), the VUE selects
\begin{equation}
    k^{\star}_{b,u,n}
    =
    \underset{k\in\mathcal{C}_{b,u,n}}{\arg\max}\;
    \widehat{\gamma}^{\mathrm{CSI}}_{b,u,k,n},
    \label{eq:system-service-beam}
\end{equation}
and reports the selected beam index together with the associated beam-domain
CSI.  The report comprises \(k^{\star}_{b,u,n}\),
\(\widehat{h}^{\mathrm{eq}}_{b,u,k^{\star}_{b,u,n},n}\), and the corresponding
quality information.  For multi-user precoding, the required equivalent-channel
coefficients over the selected RF beams are reported in the same beam-domain
form.  This model separates CSI acquisition from beam prediction: sensing
determines which beams merit measurement, whereas the service beam and CSI
supplied to the precoder are obtained from transmitted CSI-RS resources.

\subsection{Beam-Management Overhead and Effective Rate}
\label{subsec:system-beam-management}

Let \(U_b[n]=|\mathcal{U}_b[n]|\), and order the VUEs as
\(u_1,\ldots,u_{U_b[n]}\).  Their selected CSI-RS beams form the radio-
frequency precoder
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{RF}}_b[n]
    =\left[\mathbf{f}^{\mathrm{CSI}}_{b,k^{\star}_{b,u_1,n}},
    \ldots,
    \mathbf{f}^{\mathrm{CSI}}_{b,k^{\star}_{b,u_{U_b[n]},n}}\right].
    \label{eq:system-rf-precoder}
\end{equation}
The beam-domain channel seen by VUE \(u\) is then
\(\mathbf{g}^{\mathrm{H}}_{b,u,n}=\mathbf{h}^{\mathrm{H}}_{b,u,n}
\mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{RF}}_b[n]\).  Let
\(\widehat{\mathbf{H}}^{\mathrm{eff}}_b[n]\in
\mathbb{C}^{U_b[n]\times U_b[n]}\) stack the corresponding feedback-based
estimates for all VUEs.  The digital regularised zero-forcing (RZF)
precoder is
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{V}^{\mathrm{RZF}}_b[n]
    =
    \left(\widehat{\mathbf{H}}^{\mathrm{eff}}_b[n]\right)^{\mathrm{H}}
    \left(\widehat{\mathbf{H}}^{\mathrm{eff}}_b[n]
    \left(\widehat{\mathbf{H}}^{\mathrm{eff}}_b[n]\right)^{\mathrm{H}}
    +\lambda_b\mathbf{I}\right)^{-1}\mathbf{D}_b[n],
    \label{eq:system-rzf-precoder}
\end{equation}
where \(\lambda_b\) is the regularisation factor and \(\mathbf{D}_b[n]\)
normalises the composite precoding columns according to the transmit-power
constraint.  The full-dimensional precoder is
\(\mathbf{W}^{\mathrm{RZF}}_b[n]=\mathbf{F}^{\mathrm{RF}}_b[n]
\mathbf{V}^{\mathrm{RZF}}_b[n]\).  Substitution of its columns into
Equation~\eqref{eq:system-downlink-sinr} yields
\(\gamma^{\mathrm{RZF}}_{b,u,n}\).

Let \(T_{\mathrm{sen}}\), \(T_{\mathrm{SSB}}\), and
\(T_{\mathrm{CSI}}\) denote the sensing, SSB, and CSI-RS periodicities,
respectively.  These intervals need not be identical.  At each beam-management
cycle, the BS uses the most recent sensing observation together with the
communication measurements available at the decision instant.

Conventional beam management first sweeps an SSB codebook
\(\mathcal{F}^{\mathrm{SSB}}_b\) to identify a coarse serving direction and
then transmits CSI-RS over a refined candidate set.  The system employs
\(M_{\mathrm{SSB}}=48\) SSB beams and \(M_{\mathrm{CSI}}=192\) CSI-RS beams;
each CSI-RS beam has an SSB parent
\(\varphi_b(k)\in\{1,\ldots,M_{\mathrm{SSB}}\}\).  The proposed framework
retains this measurement sequence but uses an accepted target-to-user
association and its beam-prediction scores to reduce the CSI-RS candidate set.
When the association or sensing evidence is unavailable, conventional
refinement is used.  Thus, sensing changes the number and ordering of CSI-RS
measurements, not the requirement that the final service beam be measured.

Within a beam-management cycle, the normalised downlink resource budget is
partitioned as
\begin{equation}
    \tau_{b,n}^{\mathrm{tot}}
    =
    \tau_{b,n}^{\mathrm{SSB}}
    +\tau_{b,n}^{\mathrm{CSI\text{-}RS}}
    +\tau_{b,n}^{\mathrm{ctrl}}
    +\tau_{b,n}^{\mathrm{data}}.
    \label{eq:system-resource-budget}
\end{equation}
For the set \(\mathcal{E}_b[n]\) of beam-acquisition events in the cycle, the
CSI-RS resource consumption and remaining data fraction are represented by
\begin{equation}
    \tau_{b,n}^{\mathrm{CSI\text{-}RS}}
    =
    \sum_{e\in\mathcal{E}_b[n]}
    \left\lceil\frac{K_e}{M}\right\rceil\delta_{\mathrm{probe}},
    \qquad
    \eta_{b,n}^{\mathrm{data}}
    =
    \frac{\tau_{b,n}^{\mathrm{data}}}{\tau_{b,n}^{\mathrm{tot}}},
    \label{eq:system-csirs-overhead}
\end{equation}
where \(K_e\) is the number of CSI-RS beams measured at acquisition event
\(e\), \(M\) is the number of beam measurements that can be multiplexed, and
\(\delta_{\mathrm{probe}}\) is the normalised cost of one acquisition
occasion.  The SSB and control allocations are independent of the rule used to
construct the CSI-RS candidate set.  The number of CSI-RS measurements
therefore affects both the selected beam and the fraction of the cycle that
remains for data.

Combining this resource fraction with the gross downlink rate, the effective
rate is
\begin{equation}
    R_{b,u,n}^{\mathrm{eff}}
    =
    \eta_{b,n}^{\mathrm{data}}
    B_b\eta_b^{\mathrm{DL}}
    \log_2\!\left(1+\gamma_{b,u,n}^{\mathrm{RZF}}\right).
    \label{eq:system-effective-rate}
\end{equation}
This definition captures the two communication consequences of the proposed
framework: an incorrect or insufficient candidate set can reduce the
post-precoding SINR, whereas a smaller successful candidate set leaves more
resources for data transmission.  The effective rate therefore provides the
communication objective that couples sensing-assisted candidate selection to
the quality and resource efficiency of downlink transmission.
